RAW MATERIAL SECURITY — THE REAL MOAT IN ALUMINIUM
NALCO — FULLY CAPTIVE CHAIN
Bauxite → Alumina → Power → Metal
Bauxite: Panchpatmali captive mines 7.5 MTPA (leases valid to FY29/FY31, renewal process to start). New Pottangi mines — 111 Mn tonne reserves, 3.5 MTPA — opening from May 2026, feeding the 5th refinery stream. Among the world's lowest-cost bauxite.

Caustic Soda: Key refinery input. Cost rose ₹37,000/t → ₹41,000/t, but ~50% of requirement comes from the GNAL JV (with Gujarat Alkalies), cushioning spot exposure.

Coal (7.2 MTPA requirement): Captive Utkal D & E blocks produced ~4 MTPA in FY26, targeting 4.8 MTPA in FY27 (~55–57% of needs). Landed cost of captive coal only ₹1,600–1,700/t — ₹200–250/t cheaper than linkage. Balance via renewed 5-year MCL fuel supply agreement (5.6 MTPA).

Power: 1,200 MW CPP. Grid power purchase collapsed from ₹186 Cr (H1 FY25) to just ₹50 Cr (H1 FY26) — grid used only for standby. Wind (Maharashtra/Rajasthan/TN) + solar add green capacity.
HINDALCO — HYBRID: CAPTIVE + SCRAP + IMPORTED
Three Different Raw Material Models
India Aluminium (captive): Own bauxite mines + Utkal Alumina (one of the world's lowest-cost refineries) + captive coal mines feed the Aditya/Mahan/Hirakud smelters. This is why India upstream EBITDA hit a record ₹18,884 Cr (+16%) in FY26 — first-decile global cost curve.

Copper (imported concentrate): Dahej smelter runs on imported copper concentrate — earnings come from TC/RC margins plus by-products. Q4 FY26 by-product strength (sulphuric acid realisations) drove record Copper EBITDA ₹907 Cr (+48%).

Novelis (scrap-led): The world's largest aluminium recycler — high recycled content is the core input strategy. Favourable scrap prices/spreads supported Q4 adj. EBITDA/t of $544 (+10%) despite the Oswego volume hit. Scrap spreads are a structural margin driver as low-carbon demand rises.

Cost pressure flag: Consolidated raw material cost jumped 38.2% YoY in Q4 FY26 to ₹55,890 Cr — mostly metal-price pass-through inside Novelis and copper concentrate values.

🇮🇳 INDIAN OPERATIONS — EXPANSION PIPELINE
NALCO — THE BIG PSU CAPEX CYCLE
₹30,000+ Cr Multi-Year Programme
1. Pottangi Bauxite Mines (May 2026): 3.5 MTPA, 111 Mn t reserves, 50-year lease — raw material for the next leg.

2. 5th Stream Alumina Refinery (Jun 2026): +1 MTPA, taking Damanjodi from 2.1 → 3.1 MTPA. ~0.2–0.3 Mn t incremental alumina in FY27, full utilisation through FY27. The nearest-term earnings trigger.

3. Smelter Expansion (+0.5 MTPA, ~₹17,000 Cr): Targeted late 2030 / early 2031. Doubles metal capacity. Revised DPR under preparation.

4. New 1,080 MW CPP: Powers the new smelter; combined smelter+CPP capex ~₹30,000 Cr, spending ramps from FY28 on a 70:30 debt:equity structure. MoUs with NTPC and Neyveli Lignite for power collaboration.

5. Coal & Green: Utkal D&E ramping 4 → 4.8 MTPA; wind 50 MW × 2 (Maharashtra, Rajasthan) + 25.5 MW (TN) + 20 MW solar (MP).

6. New frontiers: Exploring critical minerals — Gallium and rare earth elements.
HINDALCO INDIA — $10 BN / 5-YEAR PLAN
Downstream & Value-Added Push
1. Aditya FRP ramp-up: Now contributing meaningfully to overall flat-rolled product scale-up — the downstream growth engine.

2. EV ecosystem: Battery enclosure facility at full ramp (10,000 enclosures already delivered to Mahindra); Aditya Battery Foil unit in commissioning — EV-grade foil is a high-margin niche.

3. Taloja AC Fin facility: Commissioning commenced — import substitution in AC heat-exchanger fin stock.

4. Copper: New copper tubes plant ramping; Pakhajan (Gujarat) — India's first and the world's 2nd-largest dedicated e-waste & copper recycling facility under construction; world's largest Indian copper smelter ambition stated by the group.

5. Upstream: Aditya alumina refinery and aluminium smelter expansion projects progressing to lift upstream capacity.

6. Cost programme: Permanent cost-structure reduction of $300 Mn (Novelis-led, FY28 exit), $200 Mn run-rate already achieved at FY26 exit.
🇺🇸 US OPERATIONS — NOVELIS: THE DECISIVE 12 MONTHS
BAY MINETTE, ALABAMA — $4.1 BN GREENFIELD
First Integrated US Aluminium Mill in 40+ Years
Scale: 600 kt rolling & recycling capacity, with an engineered pathway to 1.2 Mn t.

Markets: North American beverage can sheet + automotive aluminium sheet — both structurally short of domestic supply, and tariff-protected demand pools.

Timeline: Commissioning in H2 CY2026. FY27 Novelis capex $2.1–2.4 Bn, of which $1.7 Bn is Bay Minette; capex falls sharply from Q4 FY27, with Novelis guided to return to free cash flow by end-FY27.

Strategic logic: US 25–50% aluminium import tariffs make domestic rolling capacity MORE valuable — Bay Minette converts Hindalco's tariff headwind into a moat.
OSWEGO, NY — RESTART AHEAD OF SCHEDULE
The FY26 Crisis Now Reversing
What happened: Two major fires (Sep & Nov 2025) at the Oswego rolling plant — ₹4,565 Cr net exceptional expenses in Q4, shipments down 12%, Novelis FY26 adjusted FCF deeply negative.

Recovery: Hot mill restart commencing within weeks of the May 2026 call — well ahead of the previous end-June guidance — with a swift ramp to pre-fire levels. Earnings recovery expected to strengthen from Q2–Q3 FY27 as volumes normalise. Insurance recoveries staggered over the next two years.

Leverage watch: Net debt $6.7 Bn, leverage 4.1x (vs 3.7x in Dec 2025), could touch ~4.5x near-term before moderating toward ~4.0x by end-FY27. Liquidity solid at $2.8 Bn.

SECTOR-WIDE RISKS
🔴 Common Risks — Both Companies
🪙LME / Alumina Price Cycle: Alumina already normalised from $690/t (Q3 FY25) to ~$320–348/t. LME aluminium ~$2,700–2,835/t with management hopes of ~$3,000 average — any global demand shock reprices both stocks, NALCO far more violently.
🇨🇳China supply behaviour: China is ~60% of world aluminium; smelter restarts or weak Chinese demand can flood the seaborne market and crush realisations.
🇺🇸US tariffs: US aluminium import tariffs reshape trade flows — a headwind for exporters into the US, but a structural tailwind for Novelis' domestic Bay Minette capacity.
⚡Energy transition costs: Aluminium is energy-intensive; carbon pricing/CBAM in Europe will increasingly differentiate low-carbon (recycled, hydro) metal from coal-based metal.
🏢 Company-Specific Risks
🟢NALCO: Pure price-taker with zero hedging — FY26's record ₹5,816 Cr PAT was made at elevated prices; Q4 already showed the fade (-17% PAT YoY). ₹30,000 Cr smelter+CPP capex from FY28 will consume the net cash and add debt (70:30) — PSU execution timelines (smelter only by 2030–31) are the perennial risk. Bauxite lease renewals (FY29/FY31) need monitoring.
🔴Hindalco: Novelis leverage 4.1x rising possibly to ~4.5x before relief; insurance recoveries staggered over two years; a third operational incident would be very damaging. Bay Minette ramp risk — greenfield mills rarely ramp perfectly. Copper TC/RC margins are globally depressed; by-product strength may not repeat. Reported PAT optics (-51% Q4) can mislead screens.

Q: NALCO made FY26 PAT of ₹5,816 Cr at elevated realisations. Rebuilt at spot ($320/t alumina, ~$2,700/t metal), is the 12x P/E really 15–16x on normalised earnings — and how much does the 5th stream offset?
Build the bridge in three blocks. Q: Why does the market pay 17.7x for 14% margins (Hindalco) but only 12x for 44% margins (NALCO) — and what single variable flips the ranking?
Decompose the multiple into the three things it actually prices: earnings durability, earnings growth visibility, and balance-sheet optionality. On durability, NALCO's E is a price residual — FY24 PAT ₹2,060 Cr → FY26 ₹5,816 Cr is a 2.8x swing on volumes that barely moved; the market caps unstable earnings cheaply because the denominator of P/E is unreliable. Hindalco's largest earnings stream (Novelis, ~55% of revenue) is a per-tonne conversion fee — $544/t even in a quarter with two factory fires — which behaves like industrial toll income and deserves a structurally higher capitalisation rate. On growth visibility, Hindalco has dated, contracted catalysts (Oswego volumes Q2–Q3 FY27, Bay Minette H2 CY26, $350–400 Mn cost-out by FY28); NALCO's growth before 2030 is essentially one refinery stream plus whatever LME does. On optionality, the inversion flips: NALCO's net cash is genuine optionality while Hindalco's 4.1x Novelis leverage is negative optionality — yet markets weight durability and growth more than cash when rates are benign. The single flipping variable: a sustained LME breakout above ~$3,000–3,200/t. In that regime NALCO's operating leverage produces 40–60% EPS upgrades while Novelis' pass-through model captures almost none of it, and NALCO's multiple re-rates toward 15x on rising earnings — double torque. The mirror risk: at $2,300/t LME, NALCO's 12x quietly becomes 20x+ on collapsed earnings while Hindalco's conversion income barely notices. The inversion is therefore not inefficiency — it is the market correctly charging an insurance premium for owning the commodity price.

Q: Novelis spends $4.1 Bn at peak leverage; NALCO spends ₹30,000 Cr from a net-cash start. Whose capital cycle is better designed, and what does each IRR depend on?
Judge a capital cycle on three axes: demand certainty at completion, the price the project earns, and the cost of carrying it. Bay Minette scores highest on the first two axes: it lands into a North American market structurally short of can and auto sheet, with 25–50% import tariffs functioning as a state-built moat around its 600 kt (path to 1.2 Mn t), and Novelis sells conversion contracts — so the project's IRR depends on utilisation ramp and $/t premiums, both substantially contractible in advance, not on the LME. Its weakness is the third axis: it is being carried at 4.1x leverage with FY26 FCF negative, so 12 months of execution risk sit directly on the equity. The de-risking sequence is unusually concrete — capex falls from Q4 FY27, FCF positive by end-FY27, leverage to ~4.0x. NALCO's cycle is the mirror image: the financing is comfortable (net cash today, 70:30 D/E planned, government parentage) but the project's IRR depends on the one thing nobody can contract — the LME aluminium price in 2031, when the 0.5 MTPA smelter and 1,080 MW CPP arrive. Add PSU schedule risk (the much smaller 5th stream already slipped from Sep-25 to Jun-26 with a cost overrun) and the demand-side certainty is far weaker. Design verdict: Bay Minette is the better-designed cycle — it converts a policy distortion (tariffs) into a moat and sells certainty; its risk is financial sequencing. NALCO's cycle has the safer financing but is, at its core, a ₹30,000 Cr unhedged forward purchase of the 2031 LME price executed at PSU speed. Watch metrics: for Novelis — quarterly Bay Minette commissioning milestones and the leverage print; for NALCO — the smelter DPR, the financing structure (the Neyveli MoU hints partnerships remain open), and whether dividends survive the build.

Q: In which regimes does each outperform, does holding both hedge an aluminium view, and what one scenario hurts both at once?
Map four regimes. Regime 1 — global reflation / supply shock (LME $3,000+): NALCO dominates. 100% price capture, fixed captive costs, EPS torque of 40–60% per $300/t; Hindalco's Novelis captures little of the metal move (pass-through), though India upstream helps. Regime 4 — India infra boom with soft global prices: roughly neutral — NALCO's domestic metal demand grows ~7.2% CAGR but at weak realisations; Hindalco's India downstream value-add captures the same demand with better economics. So yes, the pair is a genuine intra-sector hedge: NALCO is the call option on price, Hindalco the compounder on volume, and their EPS drivers correlate weakly. Secondary joint risk: an India-specific shock (mining tax, export duty on alumina, power-tariff intervention) hits both upstream engines at once. A pair hedges the aluminium cycle; it does not hedge China or Delhi.
