⚡ What Pace Digitek Actually Does

Pace Digitek is an integrated infrastructure solutions company — not simply a hardware maker. It earns money across three modes: build (design + procure + install telecom/BESS systems as EPC contractor), own (retain ownership and charge capacity fees), and operate (lifetime O&M contracts). The company began as a telecom tower EPC player in 2007 and has executed a structural pivot to Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) from FY24 onward.

India's renewable energy push (500 GW by 2030) creates a structural need for grid-scale storage. Every solar/wind MW added needs storage backup. Pace is positioning at the exact intersection of this national infrastructure imperative.

⚡ Two Revenue Engines — EPC vs BOO: Understanding the Fundamental Difference
🔵 EPC — Engineering Procurement & Construction

Pace wins a contract, builds the system (telecom towers / BESS containers / OFC networks), gets paid on completion or milestones, and exits. It is high revenue, low recurring visibility — the contract shows up on the P&L once and is done.

Revenue profile
Lumpy, project-dependent. Q4 FY26 had ₹1,097 Cr because large BESS contracts were executed. Q1 will be lower unless new awards execute fast.
Margin profile
EBITDA 14–18% depending on project mix. BESS EPC slightly higher than telecom EPC. Raw material (LFP cell) cost is the key swing.
Valuation implication
Pure EPC companies get 10–14× P/E. Pace is valued at ~17× today because the market is pricing in BOO transition.
Revenue VolatileWorking Capital HeavyHigh Order Visibility
🟣 BOO — Build Own Operate (Annuity Engine)

Pace owns the BESS asset on its balance sheet and charges clients a capacity fee every month for 10–20 years. This is asset-heavy upfront but creates a recurring annuity stream that compounds over time — completely changing the valuation multiple.

Revenue profile FY26
₹558 Cr BOO revenue — this is the annuity base. Every GWh delivered and owned adds a permanent cashflow stream. MSEDCL, NTPC are long-term counterparties.
Margin profile
BOO EBITDA margins typically 20–30%. With battery degradation provisioned, the real long-term IRR is 12–15% per project — superior to EPC.
Valuation implication
BOO assets deserve power/infra valuation: EV/EBITDA 15–20×. As BOO % of revenue rises, Pace's blended multiple should rerate materially upward.

🔋 Why BESS Is The Right Bet

India added 24 GW of solar in FY26. Every GW of intermittent solar needs ~0.5–1 GWh of storage to be grid-stable. The government mandate (ISTS waiver, RPO with storage component) is pushing utilities to procure BESS aggressively. Pace has first-mover manufacturing advantage — 2.5 GWh capacity already operational, scaling to 10 GWh by October 2026.

Policy TailwindManufacturing Moat Building
📡 Telecom Segment — Steady But Not The Growth Story

Telecom (towers, OFC, power systems for BTS sites) was Pace's original business. It generates ₹21,000+ Cr of stable order visibility across BSNL, Bharatnet, private telcos. This segment provides the revenue base while BESS scales. FY26 telecom order book: ₹2,484 Cr (22% of total). It is the cash cow, BESS is the growth engine.

Mature Cash CowLow Growth vs BESS
⚠️ The Key Risk To Understand: Debtor Days

Pace's debtor days are 286 days — extremely elevated. In infrastructure EPC, government clients (NTPC, MSEDCL, BSNL) are notoriously slow payers. This creates working capital stress: Pace has to fund projects from its own cash while waiting for client payments. With a 0.09× D/E ratio, the balance sheet can absorb this — but as revenues scale 30–60% per year, working capital risk scales too.

⚠️ The FY26 Paradox — High Revenue, Compressed Margins

Full-year FY26 EBITDA declined 5.5% despite revenue growing 8.3%. This is the J-curve of a company in transition: higher costs of scaling BESS manufacturing (Lineage Power subsidiary), ramp-up expenses for the 2.5 GWh factory in Bidadi/Karnataka, elevated employee costs from new business head and technical teams, and LFP cell procurement costs before backward integration savings kick in. The Q4 FY26 margin recovery to 14.9% signals the transition costs are normalising. Management expects 12% PAT margins in FY27 — implying EBITDA recovery to 17–18%.

💡 Valuation Thesis: The BOO Re-rating Story

Pure EPC companies (infra contractors) trade at 10–14× P/E. Pure infrastructure annuity platforms (like InvITs, IRB) trade at 15–22× EBITDA. Pace sits at 17.6× P/E today — priced as an EPC company. If BOO annuity revenue reaches 35–40% of total revenue by FY28 (management trajectory), the blended multiple should rerate toward 22–25×. That's a 30–40% re-rating from pure business model change, before any revenue growth benefit.

⚠️ Bear Case

If EBITDA margins don't recover from 17.2% toward 19–20% in FY27 (management issue: Energy Business Head resigned), FY27 EPS could disappoint. At current price, a 20% earnings miss pushes P/E to 22× — not bargain territory. The working capital deterioration (286 debtor days) is the primary execution risk to monitor every quarter.

⚡ Q4 FY26 Verdict: A Compelling Transition Story, Not Yet A Proven Platform

Pace Digitek delivered a strong Q4 FY26 — revenue +60.5%, EBITDA +114%, PAT +88.1% — that validates the BESS execution thesis. The company is doing exactly what it said it would: scaling BESS manufacturing, winning PSU contracts, and building a BOO annuity base. The orderbook at ₹11,338 Cr (4.3× FY26 revenue) and CRISIL A-/Stable credit rating add to the credibility.

However, the full-year FY26 numbers are more sobering: EBITDA declined 5.5% despite revenue growth, debtor days hit 286, and the Energy Business Head resigned. These are not fatal flaws — they are transition-stage growing pains. But they mean the investment thesis is forward-looking, not a reward for demonstrated execution excellence.

The most important single number to track in FY27: working capital days and debtor days trajectory. If Pace collects faster as it scales — the balance sheet thesis holds. If debtor days reach 320+, the cash cycle could overwhelm even a pristine balance sheet.

At 17.6× P/E and 9.2× EV/EBITDA, Pace is priced as an EPC company when its ambition (and early evidence) suggests it is becoming an Energy Infrastructure Platform. The gap between those two valuation paradigms is where the investment opportunity lies — if execution holds.
