🎓 Hospital Sector School Fundamentals · Metrics · Global Context · NH vs Apollo Contrast
🏥 What Makes Hospital Stocks Unique

Hospitals are recession-proof, inflation-beneficiary, asset-heavy, and people-intensive. Sick people don't defer cardiac surgery during recessions. Pricing power compounds as brand builds over decades. A hospital in Mumbai cannot be replicated in Mysore — geography is a permanent moat.

India is in a healthcare golden decade (FY26–FY35): rising income → insurance penetration → more elective procedures → higher ARPOB → margin expansion. This is structural, not cyclical.

📉 The J-Curve — Beds Are Like Factories

Every new hospital loses money for 2–3 years. ₹1 Cr/bed capex. Year 1: 30% occupancy = losses. Year 3: 55% = breakeven. Year 5+: 70%+ = ₹12–15L EBITDA/bed/year. KIMS is the clearest example of this in FY26 — revenue surged 28% but PAT fell 42% due to 4 new hospitals in ramp-up. This is NOT a failure — it's the J-curve working.

🔑 Why EBITDA % Comparisons Are Misleading

Apollo consolidated EBITDA ~15% includes the pharmacy business (8.8% margin). Hospital business alone: 24.4%. Max consolidated EBITDA: 25–26.8% — apples to oranges vs Apollo. To compare hospitals fairly, use the hospital segment EBITDA: Apollo 24.4% vs Max 25–26.8% — now they're nearly equal. Fortis hospital margin 22.2% and improving. NH blended 22.7% but Cayman alone is 25–35%.

⚠️ KIMS — J-Curve or Fundamental Problem?

PAT fell 42% in FY26. Is KIMS broken? NO. Mature Telangana cluster: EBITDA margin ~28%+. Revenue grew 28.2%. The losses are from 4 new hospitals in Bangalore, Kerala, Thane, Maharashtra — all in J-curve Year 1-2. Interest costs doubled from expansion debt. When these hospitals reach 55%+ occupancy (FY27–28), KIMS PAT will more than double. Net debt ~₹2,850 Cr is high but QIP planned.

🌐 NH's 44% Revenue Jump — What's Real?

NH India grew only 10.3% organically. The 44% headline includes: (1) Cayman +59.7% driven by insurance revenue explosion (+588% YoY — Cayman now has insurance business generating USD revenue). (2) UK acquired Nov 2025 — ₹1,299 Cr in just 5 months. The UK NHS outsourcing thesis is working. By FY27, UK will contribute 12 full months (vs 5 months FY26), adding another ₹1,800+ Cr revenue boost.

🔴 KIMS — Debt Is the #1 Risk

Net debt ~₹2,850 Cr on EBITDA of ₹828 Cr = 3.4× leverage. This is the highest in the peer group. Interest costs more than doubled YoY (₹19 Cr → ₹45 Cr/quarter). KIMS is planning a QIP to raise equity capital to reduce debt. Until QIP happens and new hospitals hit breakeven (~FY27–28), the earnings trajectory will remain under pressure. Do NOT compare KIMS's current P/E to peers — use EV/mature-bed instead.

🟡 NH — Strategic Debt, Not Distress

NH's net debt of ₹2,240 Cr rose sharply from ₹530 Cr in FY25. This is entirely from the UK Practice Plus Group acquisition (US$117 Mn + GBP £150 Mn). The UK business generated ₹1,299 Cr revenue in just 5 months (annualised ₹3,100 Cr). EBITDA at 10.4% = ₹322 Cr annualised. If UK EBITDA improves to 15–18% (management target), the debt pays for itself in 5–6 years. Operating cash flow strong at ₹1,620 Cr. This is investment-grade growth debt.

🟢 Max + Rainbow + Medanta — Fortress Balance Sheets

Max net debt just ₹1,908 Cr on EBITDA ₹2,638 Cr = 0.7× — near net-cash. Free cash from operations ₹1,541 Cr FY26. Medanta net cash ₹590 Cr — self-funds Noida losses from internal accruals. Rainbow net cash despite active expansion (Bengaluru, Rajahmundry, Gurugram). These three companies have financial firepower to accelerate M&A at any time. Max recently acquired Kalinga Hospital Bhubaneswar (May 2026) without straining balance sheet.

🔵 Apollo — The Ecosystem Architecture Story

Apollo is the only hospital stock where the headline EBITDA margin (15%) understates the actual quality of the hospital business (24.4%). The integrated pharmacy + digital layer is a strategic bet that is now delivering — HealthCo digital profit up 254% YoY. The upcoming HealthCo demerger-listing in FY27 is the single biggest potential catalyst in Indian healthcare — it will allow the market to value the hospital business (currently blended and obscured) at its true 24.4% EBITDA quality. Proton therapy remains a decade-long structural moat. Balance sheet at 0.9× EBITDA is comfortable. The story here is complexity turning into clarity.

🟣 Max Healthcare — The Operational Excellence Benchmark

Max is India's operationally finest hospital company — ARPOB ₹77,900 (sector best), 75% occupancy (sector best), 26.8% Q4 EBITDA margin, net debt only ₹1,908 Cr on ₹2,638 Cr EBITDA. KKR's complete exit in January 2026 at 5× return is now permanently behind the stock. The business is exceptionally well-run. The central question for any investor is whether the current multiple (EV/network EBITDA ~38×) already reflects all the good news or whether brownfield additions (Mohali, Nanavati, BKC) represent incremental earnings not yet in the price. The clinician cost inflation (+230bps Q4) is the one variable to watch carefully.

🟦 NH — The Multi-Geography Transformation

NH in FY26 is no longer the company it was even two years ago. It now earns in three currencies, operates hospitals on three continents, and has a Cayman insurance business scaling at 5.88× YoY. The UK Practice Plus Group acquisition (Nov 2025) adds ₹1,800+ Cr annualised revenue — FY27 will be the first year showing 12 full months vs 5 months in FY26. The debt (₹2,240 Cr, 1.25× EBITDA) is foreign-currency denominated acquisition debt that the UK business itself will service. India organic growth at only 10.3% is the one modest note. NH is now fundamentally a global healthcare group that happens to be listed in India — and the market hasn't fully priced that yet.

🟢 Fortis — The Margin Inflection Story

Fortis's FY26 numbers tell a simple story: EBITDA margin went from 20.4% to 22.8% (+240bps) in one year. Agilus Diagnostics margin went from 17.7% to 23.6% (+590bps). Hospital occupancy: 68%, beds occupied +15% YoY. The IHH strategic relationship is delivering on international patient flow (₹639 Cr, +18.5%). The question is: can Fortis close the remaining gap to Max (22.8% vs 26.8%)? Every 100bps of further improvement adds significant PAT and stock re-rating. Net debt at 1.09× EBITDA from acquisitions is the constraint — if Fortis maintains EBITDA growth at 20%+ without further debt, the trajectory is very compelling.

🟠 KIMS — The J-Curve Patience Test

KIMS's FY26 numbers are the most deceptive in the sector. PAT down 42%. Interest costs doubled. Margins fell 560bps. And yet: revenue grew 28.2%, the mature Telangana cluster still earns 28%+ EBITDA, and the company just opened 4 hospitals across Bangalore, Kerala, Thane, and Maharashtra — high-ARPOB markets where KIMS had zero presence before. Every one of those new hospitals is in J-curve Year 1–2 and dragging blended margins. The planned QIP to reduce debt is the key near-term event. Investors assessing KIMS today are essentially betting on whether: (a) the new hospitals ramp as KIMS's historical hospitals have, and (b) QIP happens at fair pricing. The debt position at ₹2,850 Cr / 3.4× EBITDA is real and demands respect.

🔴 Rainbow — The NICU Compounder

Rainbow's 12.3% revenue growth in FY26 looks modest but deserves context: the paediatric business is inherently seasonal (Q2 best) and Rainbow was simultaneously ramping 3 new hospitals in Bangalore and adding Gurugram. EBITDA margin at ~30% (Q4: 32%) is among the highest in the sector for a pure-play hospital. Net cash balance sheet. Founder-led with no PE overhang. The NICU insurance coverage expansion is a structural tailwind — as health insurance policies increasingly cover neonatal ICU stays, Rainbow's payor mix will shift from 40% cash to more insurance, smoothing revenue seasonality. Gurugram (400 beds over FY26–27) is the medium-term growth story.

🟡 Medanta — The Carlyle Overhang vs Noida Opportunity

Medanta has the cleanest balance sheet in the mid-cap hospital space (net cash ₹590 Cr) and the highest mature hospital EBITDA margins (32.4% ex-Noida developing units). The Noida hospital (382 beds, ₹78 Cr EBITDA loss in FY26) is a temporary drag that will reverse — when Noida hits 55%+ occupancy by FY28, it will contribute ₹250+ Cr EBITDA annually. The Carlyle ~62% overhang is the key structural concern: when Carlyle exits, the market will absorb a large block. Historical precedent (KKR-Max): large PE exits create short-term volatility followed by re-rating as ownership diversifies. Medanta is on the same trajectory.

🔷 Jupiter — The MMR Long Game

Jupiter's PAT was essentially flat in FY26 (₹194 Cr vs ₹194 Cr) despite 15.2% revenue growth — because the Dombivli hospital opened Feb 2026 and started contributing interest costs. This is precisely the J-curve working. Jupiter's strategic position in Mumbai Metropolitan Region (Thane anchor, Dombivli new, BKC approved) gives it access to India's most valuable healthcare catchment: 25 Mn urban, high-income population.
