Key Themes Q4 FY26

Premiumisation of Demand

Per Knight Frank India, homes priced above ₹1 crore made up 50% of total residential sales across the top eight cities in 2025, up from 44% a year earlier. This is visible across the dashboard: Oberoi Realty's FY26 bookings rose in value even as unit volumes fell 25% — buyers are purchasing fewer, larger, costlier homes. Sobha, Prestige and Brigade all report average realisation gains of 7-9% YoY even where unit volumes were flat or down.

Divergent Bookings Trajectories

FY26 bookings ranged from +76% (Prestige) and +30% (Sobha) on one end to -5% (Brigade, DLF) on the other — within the same demand environment. The differentiator across this set is largely execution: companies that got RERA approvals and launches out on time captured the premiumisation wave; those facing approval delays (Brigade explicitly flagged this; DLF's Gurugram-Sector-63A launch slipped at Sobha too) lost a year of bookings momentum.

Diversification Beyond Pure-Play Residential

Three of the seven listed names in this set are visibly diversifying: DLF leans on its DCCDL annuity/rental business (₹89,780 Cr portfolio), Oberoi Realty runs hospitality (Westin Mumbai, Oberoi Mall) alongside residential, and Anant Raj is pivoting a meaningful share of its growth narrative to data centres. This mirrors a broader sector shift toward REITs, SM REITs and annuity income that reduces reliance on lumpy project-completion accounting.

Why Bookings and Reported Revenue Move Differently

The Accounting Mechanic

Under Ind AS 115, most Indian developers recognise residential revenue either on a percentage-of-completion basis or, increasingly, only at the point control transfers to the buyer (often at handover/possession). A unit "booked" today may not contribute a single rupee to reported revenue for 2-3 years until construction milestones are hit. This is why Ganesh Housing's revenue fell 62% YoY in Q4 FY26 purely on project-completion timing, and why Oberoi Realty's own investor disclosures explicitly flag that "real estate revenue recognition is not strictly comparable quarter-to-quarter."

What This Means for Reading These Companies
  • Use bookings/pre-sales to judge current demand momentum and brand strength
  • Use collections to judge near-term cash flow health and balance-sheet capacity
  • Use P&L revenue/PAT to judge what's actually flowing through to reported earnings this period — which can lag bookings by years
  • A company can have record bookings (Prestige, Sobha) and a company can have collapsing reported revenue (Ganesh Housing) in the very same quarter without either being a red flag in isolation — context (launch pipeline, project staging) matters more than any single metric

Structural Demand Themes Shaping FY26

Premiumisation

Homes priced above ₹1 crore made up 50% of total residential sales across the top eight cities in 2025 (Knight Frank India), up from 44% a year earlier. Luxury home sales (₹4 Cr+) rose ~28% YoY across seven major cities. India's HNI population crossed 850,000 in 2024 and is projected to nearly double to 1.65 million by 2027 — a structural tailwind for the premium-focused names in this set (Oberoi Realty, Sobha, Prestige's luxury launches).

Infrastructure-Led Micro-Market Creation

Projects like the Dwarka Expressway, Golf Course Extension Road and metro expansions across Delhi, Bengaluru, Hyderabad and Pune are converting peripheral land into investable micro-markets. This directly supports the land-banking strategies of Brigade, Sobha and DLF, and explains why Tier-2 cities are increasingly cited as the "next growth engine" by industry voices.

Institutionalisation & Alternative Assets

Beyond pure residential/office, data centres (Anant Raj), hospitality (Oberoi, Prestige spinning out via IPO), and senior living (Hiranandani) are emerging as distinct asset classes attracting dedicated capital. India's data-centre market scaled past 1,300 MW capacity in 2025 (Colliers) — more than 2x in five years — directly relevant to Anant Raj's strategic pivot.

Approval Delays — A Cross-Cutting Challenge

Project approval delays surfaced as an explicit, named challenge across multiple companies in this dashboard this quarter — not an isolated company-specific issue. Brigade management directly attributed its 5% FY26 bookings decline to "ongoing delays in obtaining project approvals." Sobha's Gurugram Sector-63A project slipped into FY27 specifically due to a delayed RERA approval — Antique Stock Broking estimates this alone cost Sobha ₹400-900 Cr of FY26 bookings that would otherwise have pushed the company past its own ₹8,500 Cr target. Ganesh Housing's One 91 Thaltej launch has similarly been delayed. The National Real Estate Policy 2025's single-window clearance system is the direct government response to this pattern — its success or failure in actual implementation (versus announcement) is one of the more important policy variables to track into FY27 for every company in this set.

Valuation Observations

Earnings-Led vs Sentiment-Led Pricing

DLF and Oberoi Realty trade at the most "normal" P/E multiples (32-40x and ~31x respectively) given their large, relatively stable earnings bases. Sobha (~138x) and Prestige (~53x, though source-dependent) trade rich largely because FY26 earnings, while growing fast, are still recovering off a depressed prior-year base — the multiple compresses sharply as the earnings base normalises. Ganesh Housing's P/E is not meaningfully comparable this quarter given the magnitude of the earnings decline.

Stock Performance Has Diverged from Operating Performance

Several companies posting strong or record operating results have still seen weak 1-year stock returns — DLF -27 to -28% despite a profitable year, Anant Raj and Ganesh Housing both well off their 52-week highs. This is a useful reminder that real estate stocks have de-rated sector-wide over the past year even where individual company fundamentals (bookings, margins, balance sheets) improved — broader market risk appetite and rate-cycle expectations appear to be the dominant near-term share-price driver, not just company-specific execution.

📊 Sector Overview
Which company had the best Q4 FY26 performance among the seven listed developers?
On profitability, Prestige Estates was the standout — Q4 PAT surged 596% YoY to ₹297 Cr, capping a record FY26 (PAT +112.8% to ₹1,312 Cr on bookings of ₹30,025 Cr, +76% YoY, beating its own ₹27,000 Cr guidance). On absolute scale, DLF remains the largest by bookings (₹20,143 Cr FY26) and PAT (₹4,256 Cr pre-exceptional). On balance-sheet quality, Sobha and DLF stand out — Sobha is net-cash and DLF carries zero gross debt in its development business. There is no single "best" company across every metric — Brigade and Ganesh Housing both posted bookings/revenue declines this quarter, making this a genuinely mixed-performance set rather than a uniformly strong one.
Why did Ganesh Housing's revenue fall 62% in Q4 while other companies grew?
This is primarily an accounting-timing effect, not necessarily a demand collapse. Real estate revenue is recognised under Ind AS 115 on a percentage-of-completion or transfer-of-control basis — so reported P&L revenue reflects construction-milestone timing on already-sold units, not current sales momentum. Ganesh Housing had three exceptionally strong quarters in FY25 (revenue above ₹237 Cr for three consecutive quarters) as a large pipeline of projects hit completion milestones simultaneously; FY26 simply didn't have an equivalent wave of completions, compounded by the delayed launch of its new One 91 Thaltej project. That said, the deteriorating working-capital days (592→1,040) and four straight quarters of withheld FY27 guidance are genuine, separate concerns worth tracking — not every part of this story is purely an accounting artifact.
What is the difference between "bookings," "revenue," and "collections" for a real estate company?
Bookings (pre-sales) = value of units a customer has agreed to buy/booked during the period, recorded the moment a sale agreement is signed — this is the best real-time read on demand. Collections = actual cash received from customers against those bookings, paid in instalments tied to construction milestones — this is the best read on near-term liquidity. Revenue (P&L) = the accounting figure recognised under Ind AS 115, typically only once construction reaches certain completion thresholds or control transfers at handover — this can lag bookings by 18-36 months and is the figure investors most often mistake for "sales." A company can have record bookings and collections in a quarter while reporting flat or even declining P&L revenue, simply because its currently-active projects haven't hit their revenue-recognition milestones yet.
🏢 Business Models
How is DLF's business model different from the other six listed developers?
DLF effectively runs two distinct businesses under one listed entity. The "development business" (standalone DLF) is a conventional cyclical residential-sale operation, much like Brigade, Sobha or Prestige. But DLF also holds an economic interest in DCCDL (DLF Cyber City Developers Ltd), a joint venture with Singapore's GIC that owns ~5 crore sqft of Grade-A office and retail rental assets generating stable annuity income (₹89,780 Cr portfolio value, 95% office / 97% retail occupancy). This JV structure lets DLF monetise commercial real estate without carrying the full capital intensity on its own balance sheet, while still consolidating the economic benefit — which is a meaningfully different risk/return profile from the pure development-and-sell model used by Sobha or the diversified-but-wholly-owned model used by Brigade and Prestige.
Why is Anant Raj considered different from a "normal" real estate company in this set?
Anant Raj is the only company in this dashboard actively building a second, structurally distinct business — data centres and cloud services — on top of its core Delhi-NCR real estate operations. It uses its ~320 acres of debt-free owned land as a cost advantage to build data-centre shells more cheaply than new entrants, then either rents the infrastructure-ready space to hyperscalers (colocation) or runs cloud services on it directly. This segment is small today (₹176 Cr, ~7% of FY26 revenue) but is growing far faster than the core real estate book, and the board has formally initiated evaluation of a demerger to split the two businesses into separately-listed entities — which would let the market value each on its own appropriate multiple (cyclical real estate vs. annuity-like digital infrastructure) rather than blending them into one number as happens today.
Why isn't Hiranandani Group included with financial numbers like the other seven companies?
Hiranandani Group has no equity listed on NSE or BSE — it remains a privately-held, family-promoted company. Per this dashboard's house rule of using only audited BSE/NSE-sourced data, no revenue, profit, valuation or balance-sheet figures are presented for it, since no such audited public filings exist to source them from. (Hiranandani Financial Services has issued listed non-convertible debentures — debt instruments — but that is a separate matter from an equity listing and doesn't provide the same financial disclosure depth as a listed company's quarterly results.) The Segments & Models tab profiles Hiranandani's business qualitatively — what it does and how it's structured — using public news coverage, clearly distinguished from the audited-data-backed sections for the seven listed peers.
