Quarter in one line: Q4 FY26 was a quarter of "good revenue, bad margins" for the Indian fertilizer sector. Volumes held up — most companies posted double-digit revenue growth — but profitability diverged sharply depending on each company's raw-material exposure and subsidy-timing mismatch. The proximate trigger was the West Asia conflict, which disrupted LNG transit through the Strait of Hormuz, pushed global sulphur, phosphoric acid and DAP prices up 20–30% within weeks, and forced the government to invoke the Natural Gas (Supply Regulation) Order, 2026, capping fertilizer-plant gas allocation at 70% of trailing six-month consumption. Companies with backward-integrated phosphoric/sulphuric acid capacity (Coromandel's Kakinada plants, Paradeep's Mangalore expansion) and PSUs with strong subsidy/trading tailwinds (RCF, GNFC, NFL) navigated the quarter better than companies exposed to spot raw-material purchases or one-off plant shutdowns (Deepak Fertilisers' ammonia plant, GSFC's sulphur-cost squeeze).
Types of Fertilizers & How They're Made · Feedstock → Process → Finished Product · School-level primer, mapped to what each Indian company actually makes
Fertilizers are grouped by which of the three primary plant nutrients — Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P) or Potassium (K) — they mainly supply, plus complex/blended NPK products and secondary/micronutrient and organic categories. 1. Nitrogen Fertilizers — Urea, Anhydrous Ammonia, CAN

46% N (Urea) · 82% N (Ammonia) · 26-27% N (CAN)
🔥
Natural Gas / Naphtha
Main feedstock — source of hydrogen (H₂) and process heat. India's nitrogenous plants are almost all gas-based now; a few legacy units still use naphtha/fuel oil.
Feedstock
🌫️
Air Separation
Atmospheric nitrogen (N₂) is the second input — separated from air, no import dependence for this half of the reaction.
Feedstock
🏭
Steam Reforming
Natural gas + steam → Hydrogen, under high temperature/pressure with a catalyst. This is the most energy-intensive step and the reason gas price/availability drives urea economics.
Key Process
⚗️
Ammonia Synthesis (Haber–Bosch)
N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃ (Ammonia), the Haber-Bosch reaction, run at ~150-250 bar and ~400-500°C over an iron catalyst.
Reaction
💊
Urea / CAN Conversion
Ammonia reacted with CO₂ → Urea (prilled/granulated); or with nitric acid/limestone → Calcium Ammonium Nitrate (CAN).

2. Phosphate Fertilizers — DAP, MAP, SSP, TSP

DAP 18-46-0 · MAP 11-52-0 · SSP 16% P₂O₅ · TSP 46% P₂O₅
⛏️
Phosphate Rock Mining
India has almost no economic rock phosphate reserves of its own — 90% of phosphatic need is import-dependent, overwhelmingly from Morocco's OCP (70% of world reserves), Jordan, Senegal and Egypt.
Imported Feedstock
🪨
Crushing & Grinding
Beneficiated rock phosphate is ground to fine powder for acid digestion.
Process
🧪
Sulphuric → Phosphoric Acid
Sulphur (imported, mostly Middle East refinery by-product) is burned to Sulphuric Acid (H₂SO₄), which digests rock phosphate into Phosphoric Acid (H₃PO₄) — the single largest cost driver for DAP/NPK producers.
Reaction
⚗️
Ammoniation & Granulation
Phosphoric acid is reacted with Ammonia and granulated into DAP/MAP/NPK pellets.
Finishing
📦
Bagging & Distribution
Finished DAP/NPK bagged and moved through dealer networks — DAP retail MRP held at ₹1,350/50kg via NBS + special additional subsidy.
To Farmer

3. Potash Fertilizers — MOP, SOP

MOP 60% K₂O · SOP 50% K₂O
⛏️
Potash Ore Mining
India has zero commercial potash reserves — this is the one nutrient category that is 100% import-dependent, with no domestic production pathway at all.
100% Imported
🏗️
Crushing & Flotation
Done entirely overseas — Canada, Belarus, Russia, Jordan, Israel are the traditional source countries.
Process (Abroad)
💧
Evaporation & Crystallization
Brine evaporation/crystallization yields Muriate of Potash (MOP, potassium chloride) or, via a different route, Sulphate of Potash (SOP).
Reaction
🚢
Import & Canalisation
Imported in finished form by Indian Potash Limited (IPL) and other canalising agencies; no domestic conversion step exists.
Canalised Import
Who Handles It in India
Canalising agencyIndian Potash Limited (IPL)
DistributorsIFFCO, Coromandel, Chambal, others sell imported MOP under own brands
Policy Response To Zero Domestic Supply

4. NPK Complex Fertilizers — e.g. 10:26:26, 15:15:15, 20:20:0:13

Blended multi-nutrient granules
🧱
N + P + K Sourcing
Ammonia/Urea (N) + DAP/MAP/Phosphoric Acid (P) + imported MOP/SOP (K) are sourced or produced in-house and blended in fixed ratios.
Multi-input
⚙️
Batching & Mixing
Precise proportioning per the target grade (e.g. 10:26:26 = 10% N, 26% P₂O₅, 26% K₂O).
Process
🌀
Granulation
Drum, tower or compaction granulation builds uniform NPK granules that release nutrients predictably in soil.
Process
🌡️
Drying, Cooling & Screening
Granules dried, cooled and screened to size before bagging.
Finishing
🎯
NPK Granules (Various Grades)
This is the fastest-growing fertilizer category in India as farmers shift away from straight urea toward balanced nutrition.

Strategic / Overseas Initiative
Coromandel runs an active rock-phosphate project in Senegal that ramped up output during FY26, directly feeding the new Kakinada phosphoric acid plant. The combined Kakinada sulphuric + phosphoric acid investment (~₹1,100 Cr) is explicitly positioned as supporting India's Atmanirbhar Bharat (self-reliance) push in fertiliser raw materials, reducing reliance on imported phosphoric acid — the single largest cost line for DAP/NPK manufacturing. A granulation capacity expansion at Kakinada is next, targeted for completion Q4 FY26-27.
Subsidy Exposure & Why PAT Fell So Sharply
The 76% PAT decline despite 20%+ revenue growth was driven by a combination of higher phosphoric acid/sulphur input costs, the timing mismatch between when subsidy is booked vs. paid, inventory valuation effects, and — critically — a step-up in depreciation and interest from the newly commissioned Kakinada plants hitting the P&L before their margin benefit fully shows up. Strip out exceptionals and standalone PAT actually grew 3% YoY on a 26% larger revenue base.
Quarter in one line: Coromandel's headline PAT collapse looks alarming but is largely a base-effect and depreciation/interest story tied to its own backward-integration capex, not a demand problem — nutrient volumes grew 7% and crop protection profit grew 55%. The investment case now hinges on whether the Kakinada and Senegal investments structurally lower raw-material cost volatility from FY27 onward.

Strategic / Overseas Initiative
PPL is majority-owned via Zuari Maroc Phosphates by Zuari Agro Chemicals and Morocco's OCP Group — OCP controls ~70% of the world's known phosphate rock reserves, giving PPL a structural feedstock-security edge over peers without a strategic mining-country tie-up. During FY26, PPL commissioned new sulphuric acid capacity at Paradeep (5,00,000 MTPA) and Mangalore (1,00,000 MTPA), lifting sulphuric acid capacity 45%, and is doubling phosphoric acid capacity from 0.5 to 1 MMTPA (Phase 1 to 0.7 MMTPA at Paradeep, commissioning FY27) — the same backward-integration logic as Coromandel's Kakinada project.
Corporate Action & Credit
FY26 results reflect the completed merger of Mangalore Chemicals & Fertilizers (MCFL) into PPL (NCLT-approved, appointed date 1-Apr-2024), plus MCFL's acquisition of Zuari Agro Chemicals' Mahad SSP plant. Long-term credit rating was upgraded to AA-(Stable), expected to lower financing costs and improve working-capital optimisation. PPL also scored an S&P Global ESG rating of 76, placing it in the top 2 percentile of the global chemicals sector.
Quarter in one line: Paradeep's Q4 PAT dip was a high-cost-quarter story (rising interest costs, raw material costs outpacing subsidy adjustment) inside an otherwise excellent FY26 — the OCP relationship and aggressive in-house acid capacity buildout position PPL as one of the better-hedged DAP/NPK names against the West Asia-driven price spike.

Why Subsidy Timing — Not Just Rate — Moves Earnings
Subsidy is recognised on an accrual basis against sales, but cash is often received with a lag. When raw material costs spike faster than NBS rates are revised (as happened through the West Asia crisis), companies absorb the gap temporarily — this is the single biggest reason Coromandel, GSFC and Deepak Fertilisers all reported PAT declines in Q4 FY26 despite strong revenue and volume growth. NFL's profit swing, conversely, came from a favourable subsidy-recognition note on its imported-fertiliser trading segment.
Direct Benefit Transfer (DBT) / PoS
Subsidy is paid to manufacturers/importers (not directly to farmers) but is released only after Point-of-Sale (PoS) machine-verified retail sale to a farmer is recorded — this "sale-linked" DBT design (since 2016-17) curbs diversion but means companies must first fund the subsidised-price sale out of their own working capital and recover the subsidy afterward, creating a structural receivables/working-capital burden across the sector.
"One Nation One Fertiliser" / Bharat Brand
All subsidised fertilisers — regardless of which company manufactures them — are sold under the common "Bharat" brand with a standard bag design, intended to end brand-driven price confusion for farmers and make subsidy tracking cleaner. Companies retain their own corporate identity but the retail-facing brand is unified.

Feedstock Security & Overseas Expansion · Why India Builds Fertiliser Capacity Abroad To Feed Demand At Home
India has almost no domestic rock phosphate and zero commercial potash reserves, and its natural gas production cannot fully meet nitrogenous fertiliser demand. Rather than rely purely on spot imports, Indian fertiliser players — led by the cooperative giant IFFCO and now followed by listed private players — have for two decades invested directly in overseas mining and intermediate-chemical assets, importing the finished intermediate (ammonia, phosphoric acid, urea) back to India under long-term off-take agreements. This is import substitution achieved through equity ownership abroad rather than domestic production.

Why The Government Is Pushing Equity, Not Just Imports
The Strategic Shift In Policy Language
India's Minister for Chemicals & Fertilizers has explicitly stated the government wants to move fertiliser-supplier relationships "beyond just being buyer and seller" toward strategic partnership through investment in suppliers' own assets. Indian firms are actively scouting stakes in phosphoric acid mines in Senegal, DAP-related mines in Saudi Arabia, and similar assets across Africa and Canada (for potash) — extending the IFFCO-pioneered model to a wider set of private and PSU players.

The bigger picture: India's overseas fertiliser strategy has three layers operating simultaneously — (1) the 20-year-old IFFCO cooperative model of direct JV equity in Oman/Jordan/Senegal, which secures fixed-quantity long-term offtake; (2) a newer push, restated by the government during the FY26 crisis, to extend that equity-ownership model to private and PSU players for phosphate/potash mines in Africa, Saudi Arabia and Canada; and (3) accelerated domestic backward integration into sulphuric/phosphoric acid by Coromandel, Paradeep and others, which reduces the volume of imported intermediate needed per tonne of finished DAP/NPK. None of these fully eliminates India's structural 90% phosphate / 100% potash import dependence — they reduce price and supply-disruption risk at the margin, which is precisely what got tested hardest in Q4 FY26.

Sector SWOT & Risks · What Could Go Right Or Wrong Across The Whole Indian Fertiliser Complex

Strengths

  • Captive, policy-protected demand — India is the world's 2nd-largest fertiliser consumer with structurally growing food-security needs
  • Multiple companies now running real backward integration (sulphuric/phosphoric acid) reducing per-tonne import intensity
  • Strong balance sheets among leaders (GNFC debt-free; Paradeep upgraded to AA-)
  • OCP Morocco/IFFCO-style strategic raw-material partnerships give select players structural cost advantages

Weaknesses

  • Structural import dependence: 25% urea, 90% phosphate, 100% potash
  • Urea segment economics broken for several players (GNFC explicitly flags it as loss-making)
  • Subsidy-timing mismatch creates working-capital strain and earnings volatility independent of operating performance
  • NPK application ratio still skewed 10.9:4.4:1 vs ideal 4:2:1 — overuse of subsidised, cheap urea

Opportunities

  • NBS rate hikes (Kharif 2026 +11-12%) partially pass through rising costs
  • Equity stakes in overseas phosphate/potash assets (Senegal, Saudi Arabia, Canada) under active government encouragement
  • Nano fertilisers (Nano Urea/DAP) and biofertilisers — new, higher-margin, lower-logistics-cost categories
  • Crop protection/specialty nutrient diversification (Coromandel, Deepak) offers margin upside outside core subsidised fertiliser

Threats

  • Geopolitical chokepoints: Strait of Hormuz disruption directly cut gas allocation and spiked DAP/sulphur prices in Q4 FY26
  • Fiscal pressure: subsidy budget already tight at ₹1.71 lakh Cr; CRISIL warns of likely overshoot
  • Long-term policy risk from the government's stated 50% chemical-fertiliser-reduction ambition
  • Currency depreciation amplifies the cost of dollar-denominated raw material imports

House view (CB Research synthesis, not investment advice): FY27 earnings across the sector will largely be a function of how quickly the West Asia-driven raw-material spike normalises and how the government times NBS rate revisions against it. Companies with completed or near-complete backward integration (Coromandel's Kakinada plants, Paradeep's acid expansion) and those with secured long-term gas contracts (Deepak's Equinor deal) carry the most visible margin-recovery path. PSU names (RCF, NFL, GNFC) remain more exposed to subsidy-recognition timing and trading-income volatility than to manufacturing economics per se. UPL and Coromandel's/Deepak's crop-protection segments offer a partial hedge against pure fertiliser-cycle risk within otherwise fertiliser-heavy portfolios.
