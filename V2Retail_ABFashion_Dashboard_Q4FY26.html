🎓 V2 Retail School What the company does · Sector context · Business model fundamentals
🛍️ What V2 Retail Actually Does

V2 Retail is a value fashion retailer — it sells apparel, footwear and FMCG-type household goods at everyday-low prices, targeting Tier II, Tier III and rural India rather than metros. Originally incorporated in 2001 as Vishal Retail, the company operates a chain of large-format stores (average ~10,000+ sq ft) offering readymade garments, non-apparel and general merchandise under a mix of private labels and third-party brands.

Unlike premium fashion retailers, V2's edge is cost discipline and hyper-local merchandising — deep penetration into under-served small-town markets where organised retail barely exists, competing more with unorganised local shops than with metro fashion chains.

🛍️ Two Growth Engines — New Stores vs Same-Store Sales
🔵 New Store Rollout — The Volume Engine

V2 opened 139 stores and closed 3 in FY26 (net +136), taking the network to 325 stores across 25 states and 1 Union Territory. Capex per store is ~₹2.2 Cr (₹1.1 Cr buildout + ₹1.1 Cr inventory), with store-level payback of ~4 years and breakeven within the first month of opening.

🌾 Why Tier II/III Is The Right Bet

India's organised retail penetration in small towns remains low; unbranded local shops still dominate. V2 is deliberately clustering expansion in UP, Bihar, Odisha, Jharkhand, Punjab, Rajasthan and parts of Maharashtra and Andhra Pradesh — markets where rising disposable incomes are creating first-time demand for organised, branded value fashion.

🏢 ABFRL School — Aditya Birla Fashion & Retail
🧵 What ABFRL Actually Does

ABFRL is a multi-format, multi-brand fashion retailer under the Aditya Birla Group. After its May 2025 demerger sent the Madura western-wear brands (Louis Philippe, Van Heusen, Allen Solly, Peter England) into a separately listed entity — Aditya Birla Lifestyle Brands Ltd (ABLBL) — the remaining ABFRL now runs three distinct businesses: Pantaloons (value/masstige family fashion), an ethnic & designer portfolio (Sabyasachi, Shantnu & Nikhil, Tarun Tahiliani, House of Masaba, TASVA, Jaypore, and the TCNS brands W/Aurelia), and TMRW — a house of Gen-Z digital-first D2C brands (Bewakoof, Wrogn, Nobero and others).

Unlike V2's single-format value model, ABFRL is a portfolio company spanning value, premium ethnic and digital-first — each with a different margin and growth profile, consolidated under one listed entity.

🏛️ Why The Demerger Matters

By spinning off the profitable, cash-generative Madura western-wear brands into ABLBL, the remaining ABFRL is a purer play on value + ethnic + digital-first growth — segments with more TAM but also more investment intensity. This makes ABFRL's near-term consolidated losses partly a structural artefact of which brands stayed vs left, not purely operational failure.

Structural ReshapingNear-Term Losses By Design
💳 Interest & Depreciation — The Real Profitability Blocker

ABFRL's combined interest and depreciation burden (~₹495 Cr in Q4 FY26) is more than double its operating profit — a structural deficit that requires operating margins well above current levels to offset. This, not weak revenue growth, is the core reason for the persistent net loss.

High Fixed-Cost BurdenNeeds 25%+ Operating Margin To Break Even
⚠️ The Key Risk To Understand: Six Years Of Losses

ABFRL has now posted consolidated net losses for six consecutive years, with FY26's loss widening 82% YoY to ₹830 Cr (partly due to a one-off Labour Code charge and the loss of a prior year's exceptional gain). Investors need genuine evidence — not just revenue growth — that the path to consolidated profitability is real and near-term.

⚠️ The Q4 Paradox — Strong YoY Growth, Sharp QoQ Profit Collapse

Q4 FY26 revenue grew 60% YoY, but PAT fell 83% sequentially from Q3's ₹102.7 Cr to just ₹17.5 Cr. This is a seasonal + capital-intensity story: Q3 (Oct–Dec) captures the festive/wedding season peak with better operating leverage, while Q4 sees typical post-festive deleverage. Layered on top: depreciation jumped 46% QoQ to ₹66.8 Cr and interest costs rose to ₹24 Cr as new stores from the FY26 rollout hit the books. Investors should read V2's quarterly PAT with this seasonality and expansion-capex lens rather than expecting linear quarter-on-quarter profit growth.

⚠️ The ABFRL Paradox — Real Operating Improvement, Still Consolidated Losses

ABFRL's Q4 FY26 operating metrics are genuinely improving — EBITDA +57%, margin up ~410 bps, Pantaloons +18.5%, TMRW +45%. Yet the FY26 net loss widened 82% YoY to ₹830 Cr, partly due to a ₹39.86 Cr Labour Code charge and the absence of a prior year's ₹161 Cr one-off gain, but fundamentally because interest and depreciation (~₹495 Cr in Q4 alone) still exceed operating profit. This is a business getting operationally better while remaining financially loss-making — a distinction investors should not conflate.

⚠️ Reading The Operational Data Together — Two New Monitorables

Two data points from the FY26 operational disclosure sharpen the risk picture beyond what headline P&L growth shows. First, PSF actually declined from ₹1,017/month to ₹925/month — company-wide productivity moved the wrong way even as ABV and ASP both rose, most likely because 136 new, still-maturing stores are diluting the blended average; this needs to reverse as the FY26 cohort matures. Second, Net Working Capital days nearly doubled from 45 to 81 days, driven by inventory built up both to stock existing stores and to pre-position for FY27's planned 170–200 store additions. Combined with Net D/E of ~3.35× and interest cover of ~1.35×, rising NWC intensity is a genuine balance-sheet monitorable, not just a rounding effect of scale.

⚖️ Peer Spotlight: Aditya Birla Fashion & Retail (ABFRL) Contrasting Business Models in Indian Apparel Retail
🧵 Why Compare V2 Retail With ABFRL?

V2 Retail and ABFRL both sit in Indian apparel retail but occupy opposite ends of the model spectrum. V2 is a lean, value-fashion, Tier II/III-focused chain that is profitable and growing fast off a smaller base. ABFRL (post its May 2025 demerger of the Madura western-wear brands into Aditya Birla Lifestyle Brands Ltd/ABLBL) now runs Pantaloons (value/masstige), an ethnic portfolio (Sabyasachi, Shantnu & Nikhil, Tarun Tahiliani, House of Masaba, TASVA, Jaypore, and TCNS brands like W and Aurelia) and TMRW's digital-first brands — a much larger, multi-format business that remains loss-making at the consolidated level.

💡 CB Research Take: Two Different Bets, Not True Substitutes

V2 Retail is a profitable growth bet priced for perfection (50–65× P/E) on continued store rollout and SSSG. ABFRL is a turnaround/scale bet — much larger revenue base, brand diversity, and a visible path to profitability via Pantaloons and ethnic margin expansion, but still consolidated loss-making with high leverage. Investors comparing the two should be clear they are underwriting different risks: V2's is execution risk on an already-expensive growth story; ABFRL's is turnaround risk on an already-cheap, larger, more complex portfolio. This is analytical context, not a recommendation to prefer either stock.

🛍️ Q4 FY26 Verdict: A High-Quality Growth Story, Priced Like One

V2 Retail delivered a strong Q4 and FY26 — FY26 revenue +63%, EBITDA +77%, PAT +125%, ROE up to 26%. The Tier II/III cluster strategy is working: 136 net new stores, 8.6% SSSG, and 90% full-price sales all point to genuine demand pull rather than discount-led growth. Compared with ABFRL — a much larger but consolidated loss-making peer — V2's profitability and capital efficiency stand out.

However, the Q4 numbers also carry a caution flag: PAT collapsed 83% sequentially as depreciation and interest costs from rapid store additions bit into the bottom line, and leverage (Net D/E ~3.35×, interest cover ~1.35×) is meaningfully higher than typical retail peers. These are not signs of a broken business, but they do mean the growth is being funded on a thinner cushion than investors may assume from headline YoY numbers alone.

The most important number to track in FY27: whether interest cover improves as EBITDA scales, and whether the guided 170–200 store additions execute without further leverage deterioration. If both hold, the growth story continues on a sounder footing.

At 54–64× P/E, V2 Retail is priced as a best-in-class growth compounder, not merely a value retailer. That valuation leaves very little room for a growth disappointment — the gap between "growth continues as guided" and "growth moderates" is where the investment risk concentrates.

🏢 Q4 FY26 Verdict — ABFRL: Operationally Improving, Financially Still Unproven

ABFRL's Q4 FY26 shows genuine operational progress — EBITDA +57% YoY, margin up ~410 bps to 15.7%, Pantaloons +18.5%, and TMRW +45%. The post-demerger portfolio (value + ethnic + digital-first) is diversified and each segment is moving in the right direction. Against V2 Retail, ABFRL offers ~2.7× the revenue scale and genuine brand diversity that a single-format retailer cannot replicate.

However, the consolidated bottom line has not caught up: FY26 net loss widened 82% YoY to ₹830 Cr, driven by a heavy interest and depreciation burden (~₹495 Cr in Q4 alone, more than double operating profit) and one-off items. This is now six consecutive years of losses — a track record that requires more than "margins are improving" to change the narrative.

The most important number to track in FY27: whether operating profit growth finally outpaces the fixed interest and depreciation burden enough to move the group toward consolidated breakeven. Deleveraging — even partial — would matter more to the equity story than further revenue growth alone.

At a P/B of ~0.9–1.0× and no meaningful P/E, ABFRL is priced by the market as an unproven turnaround, not as the scale leader its revenue base would suggest. Whether that's a mispriced opportunity or an appropriately cautious discount depends entirely on execution over the next 2–3 years — which is exactly why brokerage views on the stock are so split (Jefferies/Morgan Stanley Buy vs CLSA Underperform).
