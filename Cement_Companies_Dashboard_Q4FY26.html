Business Profiles · What each company does · Clinker & Grinding · Inputs · Revenue model
🏭 Cement Manufacturing 101: Cement production has two critical stages. Clinker production (integrated plants): Raw materials — Limestone (~80%), Clay, Iron ore, Gypsum — are crushed, heated in rotary kilns at ~1,450°C to form clinker nodules. Energy intensive — power & fuel = 35–40% of cost. Grinding units (blending/splitting): Clinker is transported from integrated plants and ground with gypsum and fly ash (or slag) to produce Portland cement. Much lower capex per tonne vs integrated plants. Key inputs: Coal & Pet Coke (fuel for kilns), Fly Ash / GGBS / Slag (SCM — Supplementary Cementitious Material, reduces clinker factor), Packaging bags (impacted by West Asia conflict in FY26), Diesel (logistics), Power (captive preferred). Clinker factor = ratio of clinker to cement — lower is better (more blending = lower cost). JSW targets 51% clinker factor; industry average ~65–68%.
UltraTech Cement Limited — India's #1 Cement Company · Aditya Birla Group · Listed BSE/NSE
Business Overview

UltraTech is India's largest and the world's third-largest cement manufacturer (outside China). Headquartered in Mumbai, it is the flagship cement business of the Aditya Birla Group. Its product portfolio spans Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC), Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC), Portland Slag Cement (PSC), Ready Mix Concrete (RMC), White Cement, and Dry Mortars.

Integrated Plants (Clinker): 22 integrated manufacturing units across India — major clinker hubs in Rajasthan, Chhattisgarh, AP, Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh. Clinker capacity ~140 MTPA. In Q4 FY26, domestic grey cement volume: 42.41 MT with 89% utilisation — highest in sector.

Grinding Units: 27 grinding units spread across India to serve regional markets efficiently. Reduces logistics cost vs transporting clinker long distances. Fly ash/slag blending happens here — supports "green cement."

Key Inputs: Coal (domestic + imported) + Pet Coke for kilns. Efficiency gains of ₹185/T achieved in FY26. Alternative fuels being scaled. WHRS (Waste Heat Recovery Systems) at major plants reduce power cost by ₹40–60/T.

Inorganic Growth: Acquired India Cements (Tamil Nadu) in FY24–25, adding ~15 MTPA. Reached milestone 200 MTPA in Q4 FY26.

Ambuja Cements Limited — Adani Group · One Cement Platform · BSE: 500425
Business Overview

Ambuja Cements is the flagship cement entity of the Adani Group, operating under the "One Cement Platform" which consolidates Ambuja, ACC, Orient Cement, Sanghi Industries, and Penna Cement. India's second-largest cement company. Headquarters: Ahmedabad, Gujarat.

Integrated Plants (Clinker): Major plants at Darlaghat (HP), Rauri (Gujarat), Ambujanagar (Gujarat). FY26 — expanding Maratha clinker unit by 4 MTPA (H1 FY27). Total clinker capacity across One Cement Platform ~60 MTPA.

Grinding Units: Multiple grinding units across West, North, and East India. New grinding additions in FY27: Dahej (1.2 MTPA), Bhatinda (1.2 MTPA), Salai Banwa (2.4 MTPA), Jodhpur (2 MTPA), Kalamboli (1 MTPA), Warisaliganj (2.4 MTPA) — totalling ~10 MTPA in H1 FY27.

Key Inputs: Coal imported through Adani ports (synergy advantage). Fly ash blending well-established. Premium cement products = 35% of trade sales FY26. Q4 FY26 production cost peaked at ₹4,500/T due to West Asia packaging bag costs. Target FY27 average: ₹4,250/T.

Brand: Ambuja Plus, Ambuja Kawach, Ambuja Compocem — premium positioning.

ACC Limited — Adani Cement · Ambuja Subsidiary · BSE: 500410 · Established 1936
Business Overview

ACC Limited, one of India's oldest cement brands (est. 1936), is now a subsidiary of Ambuja Cements and part of the Adani Cement portfolio. Products: OPC, PPC, Bulk cement, Ready Mix Concrete (RMC), and specialty cements including ACC Gold, ACC Suraksha.

Integrated Plants (Clinker): Key clinker plants at Wadi (Karnataka), Kymore (MP), Gagal (HP — which had notable excise duty refund in FY25), Chaibasa (Jharkhand), Bargarh (Odisha). Total integrated capacity ~30 MTPA clinker.

Grinding Units: Multiple GUs across Maharashtra (Thane, Katni), West Bengal (Tikaria), Rajasthan (Sindri), Kerala (Kochi) — ensures pan-India reach with lower logistics cost.

Key Inputs: Coal/petcoke (Q4 FY26 power & fuel costs rose to ₹1,314/T, up 4% YoY). Fly ash blending (eastern India advantage). Q4 PAT took a one-time hit compared to exceptional Gagal plant income in Q4 FY25 — base effect explains −68% PAT YoY.

RMC Business: ₹573.82 Cr revenue in Q4 FY26 — up 36.6% YoY. Growing urban construction demand fuels this segment.

Shree Cement Limited — India's #3 Cement Group · Rajasthan HQ · BSE: 500387
Business Overview

Shree Cement is India's third-largest cement group by installed capacity (69.3 MTPA post-Kodla, Karnataka plant commissioned Q4 FY26). Known for exceptional cost efficiency, premium positioning, and a "value over volume" philosophy. Promoter: B.G. Bangur family.

Integrated Plants (Clinker): Major integrated plants at Beawar (Rajasthan), Ras (Rajasthan), Raipur (Chhattisgarh), Aurangabad (Bihar), Kodla (Karnataka — NEW FY26: 3.65 MTPA clinker). Rajasthan plants benefit from captive limestone mines. WHRS at all major plants.

Grinding Units: GUs in Kolkata (WB), Panipat (Haryana), Jobner (Rajasthan), Bulandshahr (UP), Baloda Bazar (Chhattisgarh), Guntur (AP). Recent AP GU now serves south India market.

Key Inputs: Petcoke (dominant fuel — historically cheaper than coal; exposed to price volatility). Green electricity = 61% in Q4 FY26, capacity 666.5 MW. Fly ash blending. Premium products = 22% of trade sales (up from 16% in Q4 FY25).

Power Business: Sells excess power from captive plants. WHRS generates ~₹200–250 Cr annually. All plants are Zero Liquid Discharge. Among India's Top 60 Most Sustainable Companies.

JSW Cement Limited — JSW Group · Listed Aug 2025 · BSE/NSE · CEO: Nilesh Narwekar
Business Overview

JSW Cement, the newest listed entity in this peer group (IPO August 14, 2025), is part of the JSW Group. It has a unique product differentiation — cement + GGBS (Ground Granulated Blast-Furnace Slag) — leveraging JSW Steel's slag output as raw material. This gives structural cost and green credentials advantage.

Integrated Plants (Clinker): New Nagaur integrated greenfield plant (Rajasthan) — commissioned March 2026: 3.3 MTPA clinkerization + 2.5 MTPA grinding. This marks JSW's entry into North India. Additional 2.5 MTPA grinding being added at Nagaur (₹430 Cr capex approved). Prior plants: Nandyal (AP), Shiva Cement (Odisha subsidiary, listed) — integrated.

Grinding Units: Vijayanagar (Karnataka — adjacent to JSW Steel plant), Dolvi (Maharashtra), Salboni (WB). GUs are strategically co-located with JSW Steel plants — free access to slag for GGBS. Shiva Cement commissioned new 1 MTPA GU East India.

GGBS Advantage: FY26 GGBS volume = 5.78 MT (up 12%). GGBS is a green substitute for clinker in PSC — reduces carbon footprint by ~40% vs OPC. JSW's clinker factor of 51% is among the lowest in India — structural cost advantage.

Key Inputs: Limestone (own quarries + purchased), Slag from JSW Steel (captive advantage), Pet Coke/Coal for Nagaur kiln. Power: captive power at Vijayanagar.

🎓 Cement School · Everything you need to know about the cement industry · EIL Research
Why Cement Matters More Than You Think
Cement is not a commodity — it is the backbone of every road, bridge, dam, home, and factory in India. Every ₹1 lakh crore spent on infrastructure requires roughly 0.8–1 MT of cement. India consumes ~420 MT per year, second only to China (~2,000 MT). With urbanisation at 36% (target: 50% by 2047) and a ₹11 lakh crore annual infrastructure budget, demand will only grow. Understanding how cement is made, priced, and competed for is understanding India's growth story itself.
Chapter 1 — Clinker: The Heart of Cement
The Most Critical Asset in the Entire Value Chain
What Is Clinker?

Clinker is the intermediate product formed when limestone, clay, iron ore, and silica are blended and burned at 1,450°C in a rotary kiln. It emerges as dark grey pellets — the actual "binding agent" that gives cement its strength.

Think of clinker as the baked dough and cement as the finished bread. You can bake at one location, transport the dough, and finish the bread at a bakery near the customer. That logic is exactly how integrated plants + grinding units work.

Raw materials needed per tonne of clinker:
🪨 Limestone — ~1.5 tonnes (80% of mix)
🟫 Clay / Shale — ~0.25 tonnes
🔩 Iron ore / Laterite — ~0.05 tonnes
🧂 Gypsum — added at grinding stage (regulates setting time)
Why Clinker Capacity = Strategic Power

Whoever controls clinker capacity controls cement supply. You cannot make cement without clinker. A grinding unit without its own clinker supply is permanently dependent — either buying clinker from a competitor or paying freight from a distant integrated plant.

Why Companies Race to Build Grinding Units

The golden rule in cement: freight cost = 15–22% of total cost. Cement is heavy, low value-to-weight. Transporting cement 500 km by road costs ₹250–350/tonne — nearly 6–8% of the selling price wiped out just in logistics.

A grinding unit placed 100 km from the target market converts a distant cement company into a local supplier. This is why UltraTech has 27 grinding units — they can serve almost any city in India with a local price advantage.

Key Insight: Ambuja's 10 new grinding units in H1 FY27 (Dahej, Bhatinda, Salai Banwa, Jodhpur etc.) are not expansion for the sake of size — they are a deliberate freight cost reduction strategy targeting markets where UltraTech currently has a logistics advantage.
Chapter 3 — Raw Materials: The Hidden Margin Drivers
What Goes In Determines What Comes Out — in Cost and Quality
🪨 Limestone — The Crown Jewel

Limestone is the single most important raw material — and it is non-movable. You cannot import limestone economically. This is why cement plants are always built near limestone deposits.

Companies with long-term mining leases have a permanent structural cost advantage. Shree Cement's Rajasthan mines, Ramco's Ariyalur mines in Tamil Nadu, and UltraTech's mines in Rajasthan/AP are strategic moats that a new entrant simply cannot replicate overnight.

India has ~100 billion tonnes of limestone reserves. But only high-grade deposits near markets are truly valuable.
🌫️ Fly Ash & Slag — The Margin Enhancers

Fly ash is a by-product of coal-fired power plants. GGBS (Ground Granulated Blast-furnace Slag) is a steel industry by-product. Both can partially replace clinker in cement — reducing cost by ₹200–400/tonne.

JSW Cement's unique advantage: being part of JSW Steel, it has captive GGBS supply at near-zero cost. This is why JSW achieves a clinker factor of 51% — industry average is 65–68%. That 14% difference = ₹250–350/T lower cost.

Fly ash availability is tied to thermal power plant locations. Eastern India (near NTPC plants) = natural fly ash advantage for ACC, UltraTech.
📦 Packaging Bags — The Overlooked Cost

Every tonne of cement sold in bags needs 20 HDPE/PP bags (50 kg each). Bag cost = ₹4–5 per bag = ₹80–100/tonne, or roughly 2% of cost.

In FY26, West Asia conflict disrupted polypropylene supply chains — PP prices spiked 18–22%. This was a direct hit on margins across the sector. Ambuja's production cost peaked at ₹4,500/T partly due to this.

Bulk cement (supplied via tankers to large infra projects) avoids bag cost entirely — and is growing as NHAI, metro, and port projects scale up.
Chapter 4 — Power & Fuel: The Biggest Cost Lever
35–40% of Total Production Cost. The Battlefield for Margins.
Where Energy is Consumed

Cement is one of India's most energy-intensive industries. The kiln must run at 1,450°C continuously — stopping and restarting a kiln costs crores and damages the lining. Power and fuel together consume 35–40% of total production cost.

The Green Power Race & WHRS Advantage

Waste Heat Recovery Systems (WHRS) capture the hot gases exiting the kiln and pre-heater and convert them into electricity — at near-zero marginal cost. A modern 4 MTPA plant can generate 15–20 MW of free power through WHRS.

Shree Cement pioneered WHRS adoption in India — it now generates 61% of its power from green sources (WHRS + solar + wind). This saves approximately ₹80–120/tonne vs buying grid power.

Ramco's Wind Power Moat: ~600 MW of wind power capacity in Tamil Nadu and AP gives Ramco a structural ₹100–150/T cost advantage in South India — a key reason why it survives against UltraTech's scale despite being 10× smaller.
Pet Coke vs Coal: Pet coke is cheaper but more polluting. Plants in states with stricter emission norms (Maharashtra, Gujarat) use more coal. A $10/T rise in coal = ₹60–70/T EBITDA hit — as seen in Q4 FY26.
Chapter 5 — Logistics: The Silent Margin Killer
In Cement, Where You Sell Matters as Much as What You Make
The Freight Math

Cement sells at roughly ₹4,500–5,200/tonne (retail). Road freight costs ₹0.8–1.2 per tonne per km. So moving cement 300 km eats ₹240–360/T — that is 5–8% of revenue, gone just in transport.

This is why cement is almost always sold within 250–350 km of the plant. Pan-India sales require grinding units in every major region — UltraTech's 27 GUs give it a freight advantage no other player currently matches.

Rail, Road & Ports — The Three Arteries

Road: Fastest, flexible, but costliest. Used for distances under 300 km and for retail market delivery (bagged cement to dealers).

Rail: Preferred for bulk/clinker movement over long distances. Shree Cement's Rajasthan plants use rail heavily to reach Delhi NCR, Mumbai, and even South India. Cost is 30–40% lower than road for distances above 500 km.

Sea/Coastal: Used by coastal manufacturers. Ambuja's Adani port advantage is unique — Adani owns major ports at Mundra, Hazira, Mormugao. Importing coal and exporting clinker via captive ports saves ₹80–120/T vs competitors using third-party terminals.

Ramco's Chennai port access enables clinker movement from Ariyalur to coastal grinding units in Kerala and Andhra Pradesh — a key cost advantage for South India dominance.
Chapter 6 — The Race for Dominance: Why Size Matters Enormously
Cement is a Fixed-Cost Business — Bigger is Always Better
Why Cement Rewards Scale

A cement plant's biggest costs — kiln depreciation, power plant, mine, WHRS, management — are largely fixed. Whether you run a kiln at 70% or 90% utilisation, the fixed costs are the same. The more you produce, the lower the cost per tonne.

Example: UltraTech's fixed overheads per tonne at 200 MTPA are roughly 40% lower than what they were at 100 MTPA — on the same asset base. This is operating leverage.

Scale also brings brand pricing power. UltraTech commands ₹200–300/T premium over regional brands in many markets. That premium is pure margin — no extra cost to produce.

Finally, scale enables cross-subsidisation — a large player can price aggressively in one region to kill competition, absorbing losses with profits from other regions. A small regional player has no such buffer.

The Capacity Land Grab — Why Everyone is Expanding Now

India's cement demand is expected to hit 600–700 MT by 2030 from ~420 MT today. That is 180–280 MT of new demand. Whoever builds capacity now — at current costs — will be the lowest-cost producer when that demand arrives.

Building a new integrated plant today costs ₹600–800 Cr per MTPA. In 5 years, with inflation, it may cost ₹900–1,100 Cr. So the incentive is to build now, carry excess capacity for 2–3 years, and reap the benefits for 20+ years.

Chapter 7 — The Adani Entry: How One Acquisition Changed the Industry
From a Two-Horse Race to a Full Battle — 2022 Onwards
Before Adani: A Comfortable Duopoly

Until 2022, the Indian cement industry was effectively a comfortable duopoly at the top: UltraTech (Aditya Birla) was the undisputed #1 with ~120 MTPA, and Shree Cement was the most profitable player. Ambuja and ACC were under Swiss major Holcim, operating independently without aggressive expansion.

The market was orderly — prices were relatively stable, players respected regional boundaries informally, and profitability was good. The top 5 companies controlled ~55% of capacity. A $10.5 billion Holcim exit changed everything.

The Adani Takeover — May 2022

In May 2022, Gautam Adani acquired Holcim's entire India portfolio — Ambuja Cements (63.1% stake) + ACC (via Ambuja's holdings) — for $10.5 billion, the largest M&A deal in Indian cement history.

Overnight, Adani became the #2 cement group in India with ~67 MTPA combined capacity. But more importantly, Adani brought something Holcim never had: an integrated infrastructure ecosystem.

What Adani Brought That Others Cannot Match:
🚢 Captive ports (Mundra, Hazira, Mormugao) → cheaper coal imports
⚡ Adani Green Energy → potential green power supply to plants
🛤️ Adani Logistics → road and rail network for distribution
💰 Adani Capital → ability to fund expansion at lower cost
🏗️ Adani Infra Projects → captive cement consumer for its own mega-projects
How Adani Changed the Competitive Dynamics

The entry sparked a capacity arms race that the industry had not seen in a decade. UltraTech, which was growing at 8–10 MTPA/year, immediately accelerated to 20+ MTPA/year additions. Shree Cement broke its "no debt" tradition to fund new capacity. JSW Steel decided to spin off and list JSW Cement.

Adani announced a target of 140 MTPA by FY28 under the One Cement Platform (Ambuja + ACC + Orient + Sanghi + Penna). This ambition forced competitors to respond or risk being marginalised.

The Price War Fear: In FY26, cement realisation per tonne actually declined or stayed flat even as input costs rose — because nobody dared raise prices with so much new capacity being added. The consumer benefited; the industry's margins compressed 200–400 bps from peak FY24 levels.
One Cement Platform Strategy — Consolidate & Dominate

Adani's masterstroke was the "One Cement Platform" strategy — merging all acquisitions under a single operational umbrella while retaining individual brands for customer trust. Ambuja and ACC brands are over 50 years old and command deep dealer loyalty.

Synergies extracted FY23–26: shared procurement of coal/fuel (saving ~₹80–100/T), cross-plant clinker optimisation, unified logistics contracts, and a single captive power procurement strategy. Normalised EBITDA/T has improved from ₹650 to ₹887 under Adani management.

Still work to do: Ambuja's Q4 FY26 EBITDA margin of 13.4% vs UltraTech's 22.3% shows the gap. Adani has the assets; execution is now the test.

What Makes Shree Different
1. Obsessive Cost Culture

Shree's promoters (Bangur family) are known for running one of the leanest management structures in Indian industry. Employee cost per tonne is among the lowest. Every rupee of capex is justified by a clear IRR before approval. This is not just strategy — it is family culture.

2. WHRS Pioneer — 20 Years Ahead

Shree installed its first WHRS plant in the early 2000s when no one else in India was doing it. Today, with 61% green power and 666.5 MW capacity, it saves ₹100–150/T in power cost vs grid-dependent competitors.

3. Premium Pricing Without Premium Advertising

Shree commands premium pricing through contractor and dealer loyalty rather than mass-market advertising. 22% of trade volume is premium products — up from 16% in Q4 FY25. This shift = ₹150–200/T higher realisation per tonne.

The Bangur Philosophy: "Never chase volume. Build where you have cost advantage. Never borrow to grow faster than your cash generation." This discipline is why Shree has near-zero debt while peers carry thousands of crores.
