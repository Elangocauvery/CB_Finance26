Key Themes Q4 FY26

Safeguard Duty Protection

India imposed a 3-year staggered safeguard duty (12%→11.5%→11%) on flat steel from China, Vietnam, Nepal (effective ~Dec 2025). Imports fell 27.6% YoY in Apr-May 2025. HRC prices recovered in Q4. This was the single biggest positive catalyst for domestic steel margins in FY26.

Q4 Price Recovery

Steel prices declined through most of FY26 but began a steady recovery in Q4 (Jan-Mar 2026), supported by safeguard duties and pickup in infrastructure demand. HRC prices rose ~8–12% from Q3 lows. Coking coal prices also eased — avg $217/t in FY26 vs $226/t in FY25, aiding margin expansion.

India's Production Milestone

India was the sole top-10 steel producer to achieve double-digit volume growth in 2025. Crude steel production FY26: ~168 MT (+11% YoY). Domestic consumption: +7% YoY. Capacity at 200 MT, targeting 300 MT by FY30. Per capita: 100 kg (national policy target: 160 kg by FY31).

Strategic Meaning of the BPSL Deal

Why JSW Did the BPSL JV

BPSL was JSW's most debt-laden acquisition. By bringing JFE Steel in as a 50:50 partner at ₹29,475 Cr valuation, JSW: (1) raised cash to deleverage, (2) brought in JFE's world-class EAF and flat steel technology, (3) partly ring-fenced BPSL's risks into a JV structure. This is a sound capital allocation move.

What JFE Brings to the JV

JFE Steel (Japan) is the world's 6th largest steelmaker. JSW-JFE already collaborates on CRGO (Cold Rolled Grain Oriented) electrical steel — essential for EV motors and power transformers. BPSL's greenfield Odisha location makes it ideal for future green steel / EAF transition. Japan's steel technology + India's cost structure = formidable combination.

FY27 Implication for Investors

With BPSL deconsolidated, JSW's FY27 consolidated volume will be lower (BPSL's ~4 MT annual capacity exits). But net debt falls materially, interest costs drop, and JFE JV earnings come in as equity income (share of profit from JV). Net effect on EBITDA/tonne is neutral to positive. Investors should model FY27 without BPSL's volumes but with JFE JV equity income.

Why Q4 FY26 Was Better than Q3 for Margins?

Reasons
1. Safeguard Duty — Price Floor Restored

3-year safeguard duty (12%→11%) on Chinese/Vietnamese flat steel imports eliminated the floor-price threat. HRC (Hot Rolled Coil) prices recovered ~8–12% from Q3 lows in the January–March quarter. EBITDA per tonne across all three companies improved materially.

2. Coking Coal Cost Easing

Avg coking coal CFR India dropped to ~$217/t in FY26 vs $226/t in FY25. Q4 benefited from lower landed cost contracts. Cost-of-production fell even as realisations rose → double margin tailwind. Tata Steel's cost programme delivered ₹10,868 Cr in savings for the full year.

3. Seasonal Volume Pickup

Q4 is typically the strongest quarter for infrastructure and construction (dry weather + government spending). India's infrastructure capex (FY27 budget: ₹12.2 trillion, +11.5%) drove higher long-product demand. SAIL volumes +11.4% FY26, JSW record sales, Tata Steel best-ever deliveries all in Q4.

Why China Dumped Steel into India

US tariffs on Chinese steel diverted massive Chinese surplus production to Asian markets — especially India, Vietnam, and ASEAN. China's property sector collapse reduced domestic demand, further incentivizing exports at below-cost prices. India's infrastructure-driven import demand made it an attractive target for Chinese mills. Steel imports to India jumped significantly in early FY26 before the safeguard duty halted the surge.

⚠ JSW Steel — Exceptional Profit
Why did JSW Steel report 989% profit growth in Q4 FY26 — is this real?
No — the 989% profit surge is not a reflection of JSW Steel's actual operational performance. The headline PAT of ₹16,370 Cr includes an exceptional gain of ₹17,888 Cr from the slump sale of Bhushan Power & Steel (BPSL) steel business to a new 50:50 JV with Japan's JFE Steel, completed on March 27, 2026. Strip out this one-time non-cash accounting gain and the normalised (operating) PAT for Q4 FY26 is only ₹3,475 Cr — which is still a solid +131% improvement over the previous year's ₹1,503 Cr, but vastly different from ₹16,370 Cr. Analysts universally use the normalised figure for peer comparison and valuation. The inflated tax benefit (14% effective rate vs the usual ~30%) also amplified the reported bottom line. Bottom line: the operational recovery at JSW is real and meaningful; the headline profit number is not comparable year-on-year or with peers.
What is the BPSL slump sale and why did JSW do it?
BPSL — Bhushan Power & Steel — was a stressed asset JSW Steel acquired through the IBC (Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code) process in 2021. It has ~3 MTPA steel capacity in Odisha/Jharkhand. The "slump sale" means JSW transferred BPSL's steel business undertaking as a going concern to a newly formed 50:50 Joint Venture with JFE Steel Corporation of Japan for a cash consideration of ₹29,475 Cr. The deal served three strategic goals: (1) JSW raised significant cash to reduce its massive debt — net debt fell from ~₹80,347 Cr to ₹53,870 Cr (reduction of ₹26,477 Cr); (2) JFE Steel's world-class technology (EAF, CRGO electrical steel) now applies to BPSL's Odisha operations; (3) it ring-fences BPSL's risks into a JV structure rather than the parent's balance sheet. The accounting gain arose because JSW "lost control" of BPSL and had to recognise the difference between the book value of BPSL's net assets and the ₹29,475 Cr JV valuation as a gain — a standard Ind AS requirement.
How should investors model JSW Steel for FY27 after the BPSL deal?
Three key adjustments for FY27 modelling: (1) Volume: BPSL's ~4 MT annual production exits JSW's consolidation — FY27 consolidated volumes will be lower unless organic capacity (Dolvi Phase-3, Vijayanagar BF3) ramps up fully to compensate. (2) Earnings: JSW will no longer consolidate BPSL's revenue/EBITDA — instead, JFE JV profits come in as "share of profit from associates/JV" — a line below EBITDA. This is positive for JSW's EBITDA/tonne metric (BPSL had lower margins). (3) Debt: Net debt at ₹53,870 Cr post-deal but FY27 capex guidance is ₹22–24K Cr — JSW will remain FCF-negative and may raise more equity/NCDs. The normalised PAT trajectory of ₹8,698 Cr for FY26 → likely ₹10,000–12,000 Cr for FY27 (ex-exceptional) is the right base for peer comparison.

Why did Tata Steel's India business report a 24% EBITDA margin while the consolidated number was only 15.5%?
The gap arises because Tata Steel consolidates three geographies with vastly different cost structures. The India business (Jamshedpur + Kalinganagar + Meramandali) benefits from: captive iron ore mines (reducing input costs), modern blast furnace technology, high-value automotive steel mix, and India's growing premium steel demand. This combination delivers the ~24% EBITDA margin. However, the Netherlands (TSN/IJmuiden) imports all raw materials, faces high European energy costs, and is dealing with an environmental permit revocation threat — making it a cost-heavy, moderate-margin business. The UK (Port Talbot) is actually reporting an EBITDA loss (−£48 million in Q4), as it transitions from a blast furnace to an Electric Arc Furnace model. When these three regions are consolidated, the blended margin drops to 15.53%. The India business is effectively subsidising the European operations' drag. This is a key reason analysts watch "India standalone EBITDA" separately.

(3) Debt reduction: SAIL reduced debt by ₹8,148 Cr in FY26, reducing interest outflow materially. Additionally, SAIL's captive iron ore mines provided cost stability when iron ore prices rose 10% in the market. The record FY26 revenue of ₹1,10,810 Cr gave strong operating leverage on a relatively fixed cost base.
🌐 China Dumping & Safeguard Duty
What is the 12% safeguard duty and why is it a game-changer for Indian steel?
India imposed a 12% safeguard duty on flat steel products (HRC, CR coil, galvanised) from China, Vietnam, and Nepal in late 2025, replacing an earlier 200-day provisional measure. It is now a 3-year staggered duty: 12% (Year 1) → 11.5% (Year 2) → 11% (Year 3). The backstory: Donald Trump's US steel tariffs caused massive Chinese steel surplus to get diverted to other markets, particularly India. Chinese mills — facing a domestic property market collapse — were selling HRC at prices well below India's cost of production. This flooded India with cheap imports, driving domestic HRC prices to multi-year lows in FY26's first three quarters. The safeguard duty immediately reduced Chinese imports by 47.7% and overall steel imports by 27.6% in April-May 2025. HRC prices then recovered 8–12% in Q4, directly lifting EBITDA per tonne for Tata Steel, JSW, and SAIL. Without this duty, Q4 results for all three companies would have been significantly weaker.
What happens when the 3-year safeguard duty expires in 2028?
This is one of the biggest medium-term risks for Indian steel stocks. When the 3-year duty expires (~late 2028), the government will have to either: (a) extend it based on a fresh Directorate General of Trade Remedies (DGTR) injury assessment, (b) convert it to permanent anti-dumping duties, or (c) let it lapse. If it lapses — and China's property sector has still not recovered, meaning it still has massive surplus steel — the flood of cheap imports could return overnight, crashing domestic steel prices. JSW CEO Jayant Acharya has already publicly called for an extended or higher safeguard duty beyond 3 years. The risk is that by 2028, new Indian capacity additions may have raised total supply, while demand may be moderate — making domestic producers even more vulnerable. Investors in steel stocks must put a 2028 "duty cliff" marker in their investment horizon.
Is India becoming a net steel importer?
Not yet, but the trajectory is shifting. In FY26 (Apr-Feb 2026), exports stood at 6.02 MT and imports at 5.62 MT — nearly balanced and a dramatic reversal from FY25 when India exported 4.85 MT but imported 9.53 MT. The safeguard duty sharply reduced imports in FY26. However, Goldman Sachs projects that India may become a net importer post-FY30 if domestic capacity additions lag demand growth (6.8% CAGR through FY32). India's steel production capacity is 200 MT today, targeting 300 MT by FY30. If JSW's 62 MTPA, SAIL's 35 MTPA, and Tata's ~40 MTPA targets are met by 2030, India should remain a net exporter. But iron ore quality constraints (shortage of high-grade lump ore), coking coal 100% import dependence, and CBAM pressures on exports to EU are complicating factors.
⚙ Raw Materials & Costs
Which company has the best raw material cost advantage among the three?
SAIL has the structural raw material advantage — for iron ore. SAIL operates its own captive iron ore mines (Kiriburu, Meghahatuburu, Bolani, Gua, Rajhara), sourcing ~100% of its iron ore internally at cost rather than market price. When market iron ore prices rose 10% in FY26, SAIL was insulated. Tata Steel India also has captive iron ore mines — covering ~80% of its India requirements — giving it the second-best position. JSW Steel has no significant captive iron ore and is increasingly reliant on market and imported ore. Iron ore imports to India hit a 7-year high in FY26, partly driven by JSW's demand. On coking coal, all three are 100% import-dependent — Australia is the primary source — so none has an advantage here. The met coke import restriction (Jan–Jun 2026) imposed a mild additional cost on all three. Bottom line: SAIL = captive iron ore advantage; JSW = most raw material cost-exposed; Tata = balanced (captive ore, imported coal).
How does the West Asia (Iran) conflict affect Indian steel companies?
The Iran-US-Israel conflict in West Asia is impacting Indian steel through multiple channels: (1) Energy costs: India's WPI for fuel & power surged 24.71% in April 2026. Steel manufacturing is energy-intensive — blast furnaces, electric arc furnaces, rolling mills all consume large amounts of electricity and coal. Higher energy costs compress EBITDA margins. (2) Shipping routes: Disruption to the Strait of Hormuz and Red Sea shipping routes raises freight costs on imported coking coal (from Australia/US) and iron ore imports. Iron ore from Australia travels via Indian Ocean but freight indices have risen. (3) Input materials: Some ferro-alloys and industrial gases are sourced from or priced relative to Middle Eastern energy markets. Manganese ore and chrome come partly from Middle Eastern trade routes. Tata Steel and UBL flagged ₹400–500 Cr cost headwinds from the conflict. For steel mills, input cost inflation is the direct negative; if global oil prices spike sharply, India's overall economy slows, reducing steel demand — an indirect secondary risk.
🚗 Auto Industry & Steel
How does India's record auto sales in FY26 benefit steel companies?
Automobiles consume approximately 600–700 kg of steel per vehicle across body panels, chassis, engine components, and structural parts. India's FY26 auto sales hit a record high across all categories — passenger cars, commercial vehicles, two-wheelers, and tractors — driven by GST tax reform (September 2025), RBI rate cuts (25 bps to 6.25% in Feb 2026), and export growth. This directly drove demand for flat steel products: Hot Rolled Coil (body stamping blanks), Cold Rolled coil (outer body panels requiring tight tolerances), galvanised steel (underbody corrosion protection), and High-Strength Low-Alloy (HSLA) steel. Tata Steel India and JSW Steel are the primary beneficiaries as India's dominant flat steel producers. SAIL benefits indirectly through commercial vehicle structural components. The EV segment creates an additional demand vector — Cold Rolled Grain Oriented (CRGO) electrical steel for motor cores. JSW Steel's JFE JV specifically targets this market with expansion to 3.5 lakh TPA CRGO by FY28.
Why does higher steel price both help and hurt the auto industry simultaneously?
This is the fundamental tension in the auto-steel relationship. Higher steel prices benefit steelmakers (Tata, JSW, SAIL) by improving their EBITDA per tonne and margins — exactly what drove Q4 FY26 results. But for auto OEMs (Maruti, Tata Motors, Hyundai), steel accounts for 60–70% of raw material cost. So when HRC prices rise 10–15%, automaker input costs spike, squeezing their EBITDA margins unless they can pass on price hikes to customers. In Q4 FY26, SML Isuzu/Mahindra reported a 130 basis point margin compression specifically from input cost inflation. Auto companies took 2–3% price hikes in Q1 FY27 in response. If auto companies raise sticker prices too aggressively, demand may slow — which would then reduce steel demand in subsequent quarters. This "margin see-saw" between auto OEMs and steel producers is a classic cyclical industry interaction. For investors: when steel stocks are rallying on price recovery, monitor auto OEM margins as a leading indicator of demand sustainability.
🌍 FTA, UK & EU Impact
Does the India-UK FTA help Indian steel companies?
The UK FTA has a modest positive impact, more for Tata Steel than for JSW or SAIL. Here's why: Tata Steel operates Port Talbot in Wales and a service centre network in the UK. The FTA's tariff reductions mean Indian-origin premium steel grades (API pipes, automotive flat products, specialty steel) can enter the UK market at lower duties, improving competitiveness vs European suppliers. For Tata Steel's Port Talbot, the UK government's green steel support (funding the BF-to-EAF transition) aligns with the FTA's industrial partnership spirit. However, the UK is not a major destination for Indian bulk steel — the volumes are limited. For JSW and SAIL, the UK FTA impact is minimal since they don't have UK manufacturing footprints and don't export significantly to the UK. The more important trade measures for Indian steel are the domestic safeguard duties against Chinese imports — those are far more impactful on daily business than the UK FTA.
What is CBAM and why does it specifically threaten Tata Steel Netherlands (TSN)?
CBAM — Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism — is the EU's carbon tariff on imports from outside the EU. It applies to carbon-intensive industries including steel, cement, and aluminium. Starting in 2026, any company selling steel products into the EU must pay for the embedded carbon in those products if the producing country doesn't have equivalent carbon pricing. Tata Steel Netherlands (TSN) at IJmuiden is already inside the EU — so it must comply with the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), not CBAM. However, TSN's coke oven and blast furnace operations are inherently carbon-intensive and costly to decarbonise. The Dutch Environmental Agency (EA) issued a notice in April 2026 indicating intention to revoke TSN's operating permits for coke and gas plants due to emission exceedances. TSN paid over €20 million in FY26 penalties. This means TSN may be forced into an early, expensive closure of its coke plants — forcing it to buy external coke (higher cost) or transition to green steelmaking (EAF/hydrogen) much faster than planned. The financial impact is material: Tata Steel's management acknowledged this as a "material uncertainty" in the FY26 financial statements — a significant red flag for investors.

Which of the three steel stocks is the most attractively valued right now?
This is an analyst question, not my personal view — here is the objective picture from broker consensus: Tata Steel appears the most attractively valued on risk-adjusted basis. It trades at ~27x TTM P/E vs JSW's normalised 37–40x, has clean (non-exceptional) earnings, a strong India business at 24% EBITDA margin, and a cost transformation programme that's delivering. Most brokers (Jefferies, MOFSL, Emkay) have Buy ratings with ₹215–230 targets vs the ₹217 CMP — so it's close to fair value by consensus but the upside from European recovery (especially if EU reconstruction accelerates post-conflict) is a free option. Bears (InCred Reduce at ₹939) cite excessive leverage and "beyond comprehension" 4x book value. InCred upgraded it to Add with ₹158 target. For conservative income investors: SAIL's deleveraging + dividend increase are positives. For growth investors: Tata Steel India business is the highest-quality play.
What are the key things to watch in the next 2 quarters for steel sector stocks?
Six key monitorables for Q1–Q2 FY27: (1) HRC/steel price trajectory: Will domestic prices hold above ₹52,000/tonne after the safeguard duty support?
