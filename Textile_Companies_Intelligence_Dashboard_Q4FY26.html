🇬🇧 India-UK FTA (CETA) — Strategic Impact on Raymond Lifestyle
ParameterDetails
CETA Effective DateIndia-UK Comprehensive Economic & Trade Agreement (CETA) signed 6 May 2025; enters into force 15 Jul 2026 — zero-duty access on ~99% of Indian goods exports to the UK.
Market OpportunityUK textile & apparel imports were $28.71 Bn in 2024; India's share is only ~6% (~$1.7 Bn) vs China $5.86 Bn and Bangladesh $3.67 Bn. Industry expects India's UK share to roughly double to ~12% over 3–5 years as CETA removes the tariff gap vs Bangladesh/Vietnam.
Segment Most ExposedGarmenting — Raymond Lifestyle's white-label export business (suits, jackets, trousers) sold to the US, Canada, Europe, Japan and the Middle East. Q4 FY26 Garmenting revenue +38% YoY to ₹342 Cr, EBITDA back to +₹14 Cr from a ₹7 Cr loss a year ago.
Management CommentaryExecutive Chairman Gautam Hari Singhania flagged the India-UK FTA as a key catalyst for Raymond's "bold international future" in the FY26 Annual Report, aiming to combine the company's tailoring heritage with India's manufacturing scale to address the UK fashion market.
Stated TargetsCompany has guided toward a 50–60% increase in UK exports and aims to double its UK market share within a year of CETA taking effect, alongside continued diversification of Garmenting export markets toward Europe/UK as US tariff-linked order flow stabilises.
Execution RiskBenefit is conditional on obtaining a valid Certificate of Origin per shipment and aligning products with UK regulatory/quality standards — exporters who don't upgrade compliance may not capture the duty-free access in practice.
Net Read for CB ResearchGarmenting is ~19% of Q4 revenue today — even a successful UK pivot lifts a relatively small segment in the near term. The FTA is a genuine multi-year tailwind but is not a fix for the larger Branded Textile/Apparel margin-to-PAT conversion problem.

🏭 Business Model, Manufacturing & Acquisitions
Vardhman is India's largest vertically integrated textile manufacturer, spanning Yarn (its largest and best-known business, ~670 MT/day capacity), Fabric (~one-third of group turnover, the group's stated growth engine), Garments, Acrylic Fibre (marketed as VARLAN®, ~22,000 tonnes/year), and Sewing Threads (via Vardhman Yarns & Threads Ltd). The group was founded in 1965 by the Oswal family and is currently led by Chairman S.P. Oswal. A separate group entity, Vardhman Special Steels, serves the automotive and engineering sectors — distinct from the listed textile business but part of the same promoter group.

🏭 Business Model, Manufacturing & Acquisitions
Welspun Living (formerly Welspun India) is the world's largest home-textile exporter and the #1 supplier to the US bed-and-bath market, founded in 1985 by Balkrishan Goenka as a small polyester-texturizing unit in Palghar, Maharashtra. It now operates a vertically integrated model from raw material to finished goods, serving 17 of the top 30 global retailers across 60+ countries. Its patented HygroCotton technology (hollow-core yarn, improved absorbency) has generated a reported $1 Bn+ in cumulative business. The company also runs a growing Flooring division and an Advanced Textiles (non-woven) business.

This geographic diversification is the company's central strategic positioning: as global fashion brands (H&M, Gap, Walmart, Target, Zara among its clients) look to reduce over-dependence on any single sourcing country — particularly Bangladesh, following its 2024 political transition — Pearl can offer a single-vendor solution spanning multiple countries, simplifying procurement for buyers who want to split volume across geographies without adding vendor-management complexity.

🔬 Fibre Classification — Natural vs Man-Made
🌱 Natural Fibres — From Nature
🌸 Cotton
India's strongest suit. World's 2nd largest producer (24% global output). Seed of Gossypium plant. Soft, breathable, absorbent. Used in: shirts, jeans, bedsheets, towels. Gujarat, Maharashtra, Telangana are key belts. India's cotton yarn export: ~$4.5B/yr
🐑 Wool
From sheep fleece. India produces ~45 million kg/yr (Rajasthan). Used in: blankets, carpets, shawls, winter wear. Pashmina (Kashmir), Merino wool. Premium segment. Global wool demand is declining as MMF replaces in cold-weather wear.
🐛 Silk
From silkworm cocoons (Bombyx mori). India is 2nd largest producer globally. Karnataka (Mysore), Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh. Luxurious, lustrous, expensive. Sarees, scarves, premium fashion. High value-add but niche market.
🌾 Jute
India = world's largest producer (West Bengal, Assam). Bast fibre from Corchorus plant. Used in: sacks, bags, ropes, geotextiles. "Golden fibre." Eco-friendly — biodegradable. Growing use in agro-textiles, packaging. Export: ~$300M/yr.
⚗️ Man-Made Fibres — From Chemistry
🏭 Polyester
Synthetic polymer (PET). World's most used fibre — 54% of global consumption. Wrinkle-resistant, quick-dry, durable, cheap. Used in: activewear, fast fashion, home textiles. Reliance Industries = India's dominant polyester maker. India 23-36% costlier than China due to QCOs.
🌀 Viscose / Rayon
Semi-synthetic (cellulosic — from wood pulp). Soft, silk-like drape. Used in: ethnic wear, lining, blended fabrics. Grasim (Aditya Birla) = India's only viscose maker. Viscose costs 14–16% more in India than import prices — hurts MSMEs.
💎 Nylon
First fully synthetic fibre (1939). Strong, elastic, lightweight. Used in: hosiery, sportswear, lingerie, parachutes, ropes, tyres (industrial). India primarily imports nylon from China. Used in technical textiles + defence applications.
🔴 Acrylic
Synthetic (polyacrylonitrile). Wool-like warmth at lower cost. Used in: sweaters, blankets, carpets, hosiery. India limited domestic production — largely imported. Growing in winter wear, replacing wool in mid-market segments.

🔍 Why India Underperforms in Global Textile Exports — 7 Structural Reasons
1
Cotton Bias in a Polyester World
India is the world's 2nd-largest cotton producer and built its textile expertise around it. However, 77% of the global textile market is now MMF-dominated. India holds only 3.4% of global MMF textile exports (2016–2021 — stagnant). Cotton exports face headwinds as sportswear, fast fashion, and activewear shift to polyester blends. India's MMF export at $4.85B (FY25) vs. cotton yarn/fabric at $7.1B — the reverse of global demand.
🌍 Global MMF Share: 72% 🇮🇳 India MMF export share: 15% of basket 📉 India global MMF trade share: 3.4%
2
High Electricity Costs vs. Global Peers
Textile manufacturing is energy-intensive — spinning, weaving, dyeing, finishing all require continuous power. Indian industrial electricity tariff averages ₹8–10/unit ($0.10–0.12/kWh) for MSMEs, significantly higher than China's subsidised rates (~$0.06–0.07/kWh). Vietnam offers special export zone rates as low as $0.07/kWh. Power cost alone can comprise 15–20% of total manufacturing cost in spinning and weaving. Unreliable supply forces factories to run diesel gensets adding further to costs.
⚡ India avg industrial: ~$0.10–0.12/kWh ⚡ China/Vietnam: ~$0.06–0.07/kWh 📊 Power = 15–20% of spinning cost
3
High Raw Material Cost (MMF) — QCO Problem
Quality Control Orders (QCOs) imposed by the government restrict imports of polyester and viscose fibres, forcing Indian weavers to buy from domestic monopolies (Reliance for polyester, Grasim for viscose) at above-market prices. Polyester staple fibre is 33–36% costlier in India than in China. Viscose fibre is 14–16% more expensive. This makes Indian MMF yarn/fabric uncompetitive vs. Bangladesh (which imports freely) and Vietnam. Bangladesh is the world's 2nd-largest apparel exporter precisely because it can import cheap Chinese MMF yarn duty-free.
📦 Polyester: 33–36% costlier vs China 🧵 Viscose: 14–16% costlier vs imports ⚠️ QCO policy protects makers, hurts weavers
4
Tariff Disadvantage vs. Duty-Free Competitors
Bangladesh, Cambodia, Sri Lanka, Ethiopia (AGOA for USA) enjoy zero-duty or low-duty access to EU and US markets under preferential trade agreements (LDC status, EBA, AGOA). India faces standard MFN tariffs: 10–12% in EU, 12–27% in US. This is a structural 10–20% cost handicap even if India's manufacturing cost is identical. Indian goods face the same tariff as China in many categories — despite India not having China's scale advantages. Bangladesh apparel exports to EU = $20B/yr at near-zero duty. India exports $4.5B at 12% duty.
🇪🇺 EU: Bangladesh 0% vs India 12% 🇺🇸 US: India 26% (Trump tariff 2026) ✅ US new tariff: India better than BD/VN/CN
5
Fragmented Supply Chain — No Vertical Integration
India's textile chain is geographically scattered: cotton grown in Gujarat, spun in Tamil Nadu, woven in Maharashtra/AP, processed in Surat, garmented in NCR. Each transit adds logistics cost, lead time, and quality risk. China and Vietnam operate "fibre-to-fashion" mega clusters — entire value chains in one city/region. India has only 2% of global shuttle-less loom capacity (NITI Aayog, 2020), meaning most fabric is made on outdated shuttle looms — slow, inconsistent, high-waste. Over 80% of India's textile producers are MSMEs — too small to invest in modern equipment.
🏭 80%+ producers are MSMEs 🪡 Only 2% global shuttle-less loom share 📦 Scattered geography = high logistics cost
6
Labour Productivity Gap — Wages Don't Tell the Full Story
India's labour wages are low (~₹300–500/day in garments), but labour productivity is 20–40% lower than China and Vietnam (Ministry of Textiles, 2025). Bangladesh pays even lower wages — ~$95/month vs. India's ~$120–150/month in organised sector. Despite abundant labour, India has rigid labour laws in the organised sector, making it harder to scale and flex workforce. High employee benefit obligations (PF, gratuity — recently amplified by New Labour Codes, Nov 2025) further raise per-unit cost. The output-per-worker metric — not just wage — is what drives competitiveness.
📉 Labour productivity: 20–40% below China/VN 💼 Labour laws: rigid in organised sector 📊 BD wage: ~$95/mo vs India ~$130/mo
7
Stagnant Export Share Despite Growing Industry
India's T&A exports rose from $11.5B (FY01) to $34.8B (FY24) — impressive in absolute terms. But India's global market share has been stuck at 3–4% for nearly 20 years. The global pie grew faster than India could capture. Peak was $44.4B in FY22, which then declined to $35.9B in FY24. Bangladesh at 6.9% global apparel share vs India's 2.9%. China still commands ~30% of US textile imports ($36B). India's $9.7B = just 8% of total US textile imports ($107.7B). India is ranked 6th — below China, EU bloc, Bangladesh, Vietnam, Turkey.
📉 Global share: stagnant at 3–4% for 20 yrs 🌍 Rank: 6th largest T&A exporter globally 🇺🇸 US market: India 8% share vs China 30%

💡 What Could Fix India's Textile Underperformance?
📋 Policy Fixes
  • ✅ Review QCOs on MMF imports — let weavers source cheaply
  • ✅ Negotiate India-EU FTA for zero-duty access
  • ✅ Rationalise GST across value chain (12%→5% on job-work)
  • ✅ Extend ROSCTL/RODTEP benefits for MMF garments
  • ✅ Expand PLI beyond MMF to technical textiles
🏭 Infrastructure
  • ⚡ Subsidised power for textile clusters (like Tirupur model)
  • 🏗️ Integrated Textile Parks — cluster approach (PM MITRA)
  • 🔧 Technology upgradation fund — shuttle-less loom subsidy
  • 🚛 Dedicated freight corridors for textile hubs
  • 🌊 Effluent treatment to enable faster environment clearance
🚀 Strategic Bets
  • 📈 Scale up MMF garment manufacturing — follow Bangladesh model
  • 🎯 Capture China+1 orders in sportswear/activewear
  • ⚙️ Invest in technical textiles — high-margin, high-growth
  • 🌍 Pursue FTAs with UK (near-done), EU, and GCC aggressively
  • 🧑‍🏭 Labour law reform — ease hiring/firing for export factories
🌟 Silver Lining — Tailwinds Building for India (2025–27)
US Tariff Asymmetry (Trump 2026): For the first time, India faces lower US tariffs than Bangladesh (26% vs 37%), Vietnam (46%), and China (54%). This is a rare window for India to capture RMG orders if capacity ramps quickly.
China+1 in Motion: Global brands are actively de-risking from China. India is a natural beneficiary — large country, English-speaking management, democratic governance, growing domestic market as a sweetener.
PM MITRA Parks: 7 Mega Integrated Textile Regions being set up across India — designed to offer cluster-based manufacturing with shared infrastructure, aiming to replicate China's Changzhou model.
India-UK FTA: Near-final. Will give Indian textile exporters duty-free access to the UK — a $3.5B market — giving SP Apparels, KPR Mill, and garment makers a structural edge over Bangladesh and China.
