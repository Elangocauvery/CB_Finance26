🏛️ Company Profile
Bharat Heavy Electricals Limited (BHEL) was incorporated on 13 November 1964 and is India's largest engineering & manufacturing enterprise in the energy and infrastructure space. It is a Government of India Maharatna-track Public Sector Undertaking under the Ministry of Heavy Industries, with the Government holding 63.17% equity. BHEL supplies equipment to roughly 53% of India's installed conventional (non-renewable) power generation capacity and is one of the very few companies globally with in-house design/manufacturing capability across the entire range of power plants — nuclear, thermal, hydro and gas. It operates 16-17 manufacturing units, 2 repair units, 4 regional offices, 8 service centres and 7 joint ventures, with a footprint across ~89-90 countries.
🏭 Two Reportable Segments
Power Segment
~71–77% of revenue · the core engine
Power
Design, manufacture and supply of equipment for thermal, hydro, nuclear and gas power plants, plus end-to-end EPC services — from project inception, design and installation through commissioning and after-sales support. BHEL is India's dominant supplier here, with limited domestic competition for large thermal boiler-turbine-generator (BTG) packages; several rivals have exited the segment over the years.
Q4 FY26: Segment revenue ₹9,509.85 Cr (+54% YoY from ₹6,192.41 Cr); segment margin expanded sharply to 19.7% from 4.9% YoY as execution mix shifted toward newer, better-priced (post-Talcher) orders.
Order book: ₹1.92 lakh Cr (+21.8% YoY); Q4 order inflow ₹30,100 Cr; FY26 power order inflow ~₹59,000 Cr.
Nuclear: Supplied the 45th Steam Generator for the GHAVP nuclear plant — the highest number by any Indian manufacturer — and is positioned as a key beneficiary (alongside L&T) of India's Nuclear Energy Mission (target: 100 GW nuclear capacity by 2047).
Industry Segment
~23–29% of revenue · faster-growing, higher-margin, less cyclical
Industry
Equipment and end-to-end solutions across rail transportation, defence & aerospace, power transmission, renewables, oil & gas/petrochemicals, water, and the emerging coal-to-chemicals vertical, plus newer areas like EV charging infrastructure and battery energy storage systems (BESS).
Q4 FY26: Segment execution ₹2,800 Cr (flat YoY); segment margin 24% (down from 31.3% YoY — a mix effect, still well above Power segment margin).
FY26 order inflow: ~₹16,000 Cr across transportation, transmission, defence, process industries and industrial equipment.
Defence/strategic: Commissioned the first upgraded Super Rapid Gun Mount (SRGM) onboard INS Nilgiri; supplies naval propulsion/fleet-support systems and specialised ISRO/space hardware. DAC has approved a Land Based Testing Facility (LBTF) for electric propulsion systems for Indian naval assets — a potential BHEL opportunity given its relevant experience.
Coal-to-chemicals: JV "Bharat Coal Gasification and Chemicals Ltd" with Coal India Ltd — producing Ammonium Nitrate from indigenous coal-gasification technology, a strategic import-substitution play.
🧬 Business Model — How BHEL Makes Money
BHEL is fundamentally an order-book-driven, execution-led EPC and heavy-equipment manufacturer — revenue is recognised as large, multi-year projects (boilers, turbines, generators, transformers, reactors) are engineered, built and commissioned, not on a steady-state sales basis. This makes the order book (currently ₹2.4 lakh Cr, 7.1x FY26 revenue) the single most important forward indicator, and execution pace (how fast the backlog converts to billed revenue) the key swing factor for both revenue growth and margin, since fixed manufacturing costs get better absorbed as throughput rises (operating leverage — visible in Q4 FY26 where 37% revenue growth drove 156% PAT growth). Customer advances (up from ₹15,300 Cr to ₹24,000 Cr in FY26) partly fund working capital. Margins are also sensitive to input-cost volatility (steel, gas, imported components) and to the vintage/pricing of the specific project mix being executed at any point in time — older, pre-2023 orders were priced thin and are being worked off, while the newer order intake carries meaningfully better economics.

🔦 Why BHEL is in the spotlight right now (July 2026): A cocktail of a blowout Q4, a record order book, and India's biggest-ever nuclear + thermal capacity build-out cycle. Below is CB Research's factual round-up of the specific triggers — this is not a recommendation.
🔦 Reasons the Stock Is in Focus
1
Blowout Q4 print: Net profit up 2.5x YoY to ₹1,290.47 Cr, revenue +37%, EBITDA margin nearly doubled to 14.2% — shares rallied ~7-10% on results day (4-5 May 2026).
2
India's Nuclear Energy Mission: Government target of 100 GW nuclear capacity by 2047 (from ~8.8 GW today). ICICI Securities flags BHEL, alongside L&T, as a primary beneficiary for reactor-island EPC contracts (valued at roughly ₹10 Cr/MW) given its domestic supply chain and nuclear-equipment track record.
3
Thermal capacity super-cycle: India's coal capacity roadmap envisages growth from ~210 GW to 420 GW by 2047 as 50+ GW of ageing plants retire and peak demand climbs toward 330-375 GW by FY32 — BHEL is the dominant domestic BTG supplier with limited competition left in the segment.
4
Large pipeline of near-term order conversions: BHEL is L-1 (lowest bidder) on 3x800MW NTPC Telangana and 3x800MW NTPC Meja-II (~₹35,000 Cr combined), plus 1x800MW DVC Durgapur; a Mahi Banswara nuclear package (~₹25,000 Cr) and the Chutka tender are also expected.
5
Defence & strategic relevance: DAC-approved Land Based Testing Facility for naval electric propulsion, commissioning of the Super Rapid Gun Mount on INS Nilgiri, and specialised ISRO/space orders position BHEL within India's defence-indigenisation (Atmanirbhar Bharat) narrative.
6
China input-sourcing relief: Government permitted BHEL to import 21 critical input materials from China to work around the RLNG/LPG/Helium gas shortage that pinched Q4 execution — JM Financial called this margin-accretive.
7
Credit-rating upgrade: CRISIL raised BHEL's long-term rating to AA/Stable from AA-/Stable, citing the strong order book, improving profitability and a stronger balance sheet.
8
Possible index reclassification: BHEL is cited among potential entrants to AMFI's Large Cap category at the next periodic review, which can trigger passive-fund flows.
9
Q1 FY27 turning point being watched: JM Financial expects BHEL to report a Q1 profit for the first time in 8 years (last Q1 profit was FY19), on ~20% revenue growth — a potential structural shift signal if it plays out.
10
Valuation debate is itself a talking point: The stock trades near ~84-90x trailing P/E, prompting a split among brokerages between those seeing a structural re-rating (better order mix, deleveraging, execution ramp) and those flagging the multiple as excessive relative to history and sector peers.
🏛️ Why BHEL Matters at Present
BHEL is not just another industrial stock — it is the single largest domestic supplier of power-generation equipment in India, with roughly 53% of the country's installed conventional generation capacity built on its equipment. As India pursues a dual energy build-out — a nuclear ramp to 100 GW by 2047 and a parallel thermal expansion to 420 GW to meet rising peak demand — BHEL is one of the very few entities in the world with in-house design and manufacturing capability spanning the entire spectrum of power-plant technology: nuclear, thermal, hydro and gas. That makes it a direct, high-visibility proxy for India's energy-security and capex-cycle narrative, reinforced by its ₹2.4 lakh Cr order book (revenue visibility out to roughly FY29-30). Beyond power, its Industry segment gives it a foothold in strategically sensitive areas — railways, defence and naval propulsion, and coal-to-chemicals import substitution — that align with the government's broader Atmanirbhar Bharat and infrastructure-self-reliance agenda. As a Maharatna-track PSU 63.17%-owned by the Government of India, BHEL's fortunes are also closely watched as a bellwether for the health of India's capital-goods and public-sector engineering ecosystem.

🏆

Q4 FY26 Earnings Snapshot: A Genuine Inflection, With Caveats

BHEL delivered its best quarter in years — net profit up 2.5x YoY, revenue +37%, and EBITDA margin nearly doubling to 14.2% — driven overwhelmingly by the Power segment, where margin expansion (4.9% → 19.7%) reflects the long-awaited shift from legacy low-priced orders to better-priced new business. The ₹2.4 lakh Cr order book (7.1x FY26 revenue) is the standout structural positive, reinforced by India's parallel nuclear and thermal capacity build-out through 2047. Counterweights: part of the margin beat came from provision write-backs and forex gains rather than pure operating improvement; FY26 order inflow actually declined 19% YoY on a high base; a gas-input shortage clipped Q4 execution; and the stock's ~84-90x trailing P/E already prices in a great deal of the turnaround, leaving limited room for disappointment. Whether Q1 FY27 breaks BHEL's 8-year Q1-loss streak, as JM Financial expects, will be an important near-term tell on whether this is a genuine structural inflection or a strong-but-cyclical quarter.

🧑‍⚖️ CB Verdict Card (Not a Recommendation)
BHEL — "Order Book Meets Execution Ramp"
The bull case rests on a genuinely re-rated order mix, a record backlog, and a once-in-a-generation domestic power-capex cycle (nuclear + thermal) where BHEL has structurally limited competition. The bear/watch case rests on valuation (~84-90x P/E), the non-recurring components of the Q4 margin beat (provision write-backs, forex gains), input-supply risk (gas shortages), and the PSU-execution question mark that has dogged the stock historically. Watch: Q1 FY27 profitability (first Q1 profit since FY19, per JM Financial), gas-supply normalisation, and whether the L-1 pipeline (NTPC Telangana/Meja-II, Mahi Banswara nuclear) actually converts to firm orders.
