Key Q4 FY26 Themes

Premiumisation Supercycle

Prestige & Above (P&A) segment outpacing overall growth across all companies. USL P&A = 90.1% of NSV. Radico P&A volume +28% YoY. Premium beer volumes +16% (UBL). Globus Spirits P&A +27% YoY; Sula's Elite & Premium portfolio now 78.4% of Own Brands. India moving from price-sensitive to value-driven alcobev market.

M&A Reshapes Mid-Caps

Tilaknagar Industries' acquisition of Pernod Ricard India's Imperial Blue business (completed FY26) transformed it into a ~20 million case IMFL player overnight — but loaded the balance sheet with acquisition debt, causing a Q4 net loss despite strong EBITDA. Sula's pending Chandon estate acquisition (H1 FY27) extends its wine-tourism moat.

FTA Structural Shift

India-UK FTA (ratified early 2026): Scotch duty cut from 150% → 75% immediately, to 40% over 10 years. India-EU FTA (announced 2026): wine & spirits duty to 20–40% over 7 years. Bottled-in-India Scotch model disrupted. Indian single malts gain EU export access. India-made wine (Sula) remains insulated from import competition due to domestic-grape cost advantage.

India UK & EU FTA — AlcoBev Sector Impact

Structural Tailwind
🇬🇧 India – UK Free Trade Agreement
Status: Ratified early 2026. In force.

Key Terms for AlcoBev:
  • Scotch whisky duty: 150% → 75% immediately; further to 40% over 10 years
  • Same reduction for UK gin and other spirits
  • Indian spirits (single malts, gin) gain improved UK market access

Consumer Price Impact: Limited — estimated ₹100–300/bottle drop for Scotch. States retain excise (50–60% of retail price), so customs cut absorbed into margins.

Bottled-in-India (BII) Scotch Disruption: Black Dog, 100 Pipers, VAT 69 bulk-imported and bottled in India. With lower duty, origin bottling becomes cheaper → BII model threatened long-term.

Industry Ask: CIABC sought Minimum Import Price (MIP) to prevent Scotch dumping at artificially low prices.
🇪🇺 India – EU Free Trade Agreement
Status: Announced early 2026 (described as "mother of all deals" by PM Modi + EU). Expected to take effect 2027. Covers ~97% goods by value.

Key Terms for AlcoBev:
  • EU wine duty: 150% → 75% on day 1; to 20% over 7 years
  • European spirits (cognac, armagnac, grappa, Irish whiskey) get similar phased reduction to 20–40%
  • Indian spirits gain better EU export access — Indian Single Malt direct beneficiary

Wine Opportunity: Monika Alcobev (distributor) sees European wine brands expanding aggressively into India. Wine culture expansion beyond metros likely.

Rules of Origin Watch: Industry monitoring that non-EU spirits cannot misuse the agreement (e.g. via re-routing). Indian government urged to enforce strictly.

Sector-Wide FTA Summary

Premiumisation Accelerator

Both FTAs force Indian consumer brands to compete on quality and innovation, not price protection. The Indian alcobev market is transitioning from price-sensitive to value-creation driven. International brands entering at lower prices push Indian makers up the value chain — a net positive for long-term sector health.

State Excise Limiter

State excise duties (50–60% of retail price) mean that even if all customs duty reduction is passed on, consumer prices change minimally. The industry — and particularly Diageo's USL — is more likely to pocket the savings as margin expansion rather than price cuts, per industry expert analysis.

Indian ISM Global Story

Indian Single Malt (ISM) is the biggest structural winner. Rampur, Jaisalmer, Godawan, and others gain European market access and compete against Scotch. Indian ISM saw 22% CAGR FY19–FY24. Radico's Rampur rated by Jim Murray's Whisky Bible. This is a "Make in India" global brand-building story.

FTA Read-Through for Mid-Caps

Tilaknagar & Globus — Limited Direct Exposure

Brandy (Tilaknagar's core category) and bulk ENA/IMIL (Globus's core) have minimal European import competition — these are domestic-consumption categories with no equivalent imported substitute at scale. FTA impact is indirect: cheaper Scotch could pressure the value-whisky segment Imperial Blue competes in over the long term.

Sula — Insulated on Cost, Exposed on Competition

EU FTA wine duty cuts (150%→75%→20% over 7 years) could bring European wines into India at sharply lower prices, pressuring Sula's mid-premium segment over the medium term. However, Sula's domestic-grape cost base and wine-tourism moat (not replicable by imports) provide structural insulation that pure import-competing categories lack.

GM Breweries — No FTA Relevance

Country Liquor is a domestic-only, non-traded category with no import equivalent — GM Breweries has effectively zero direct or indirect FTA exposure, making it the only listed alcobev name fully insulated from the UK/EU trade-deal theme.

Alcohol Regulations & Policies in India

🇮🇳 Constitutional Status: Alcohol is a State List subject under the 7th Schedule of the Constitution. Each state controls manufacturing, licensing, distribution, taxation, and retail. Alcohol is excluded from GST — states levy excise duties + VAT independently. This creates a highly fragmented regulatory environment.
National Framework
1

FSSAI Regulations 2026 (Effective July 1, 2026)

Brands must now display approximate number of standard drinks per pack (1 standard drink = 10g pure alcohol / ~30ml spirits at 40% ABV). "Indian liquor" formally defined under food safety law. RTD (Ready-to-Drink) formally recognised as category (0.5–15% ABV).

2

Advertising Restrictions

Direct advertising of alcohol banned on TV, radio, and digital platforms. Surrogate advertising (music CDs, mineral water, etc.) is common industry practice. New FSSAI labelling adds standard drink count, promoting mindful consumption.

3

Drunk Driving BAC Limit

Uniform national limit: 0.03% (30 mg/100 ml blood). Fines: ₹2,000–₹10,000. Imprisonment: 6 months–4 years (Motor Vehicles Act).

7

IMFL vs Country Liquor (CL) Classification

India's excise law separates Country Liquor (CL) — typically molasses-based spirit at lower price points, regulated and taxed differently per state, dominant in rural/value markets (GM Breweries' core category) — from IMFL (Indian-Made Foreign Liquor), which covers whisky, brandy, rum, gin and vodka styles made domestically. Both are state-excise categories with no GST applicability; full details in the School tab.

State Policies — Key Developments FY26
4

Maharashtra MML Policy (Major Disruption)

Maharashtra Made Liquor (MML) category introduced: grain-based spirits with excise duty slashed from ~450% to ~270%. Created new low-priced IMFL category. Devastated USL's popular brands and lower prestige segments in India's largest state. A 30% illegal alcohol spike expected during dry days.

5

Karnataka Progressive Policy

New Karnataka draft liquor policy welcomed by industry (Nomura: "structurally benefits United Spirits"). Could accelerate premiumisation in a large south Indian market. USL CEO specifically highlighted Karnataka as a positive development.

6

Prohibition States — Bihar, Gujarat, Mizoram

Bihar (2016): Full prohibition; violations attract 10 years to life imprisonment. Represents a massive lost market for IMFL companies. These states drive significant illicit liquor trade — 73 deaths in Bihar 2022, 58+ in Tamil Nadu June 2024 (methanol tragedy).

QWhy did USL's Q4 FY26 revenue growth (+3.7%) lag Radico's (+15.3%) despite both being spirits players?

🎓 AlcoBev School — Understanding What You're Investing In

A plain-language guide to how whisky, brandy, beer, wine, and country liquor are actually made — and why India's alcobev industry looks structurally different from Europe and America. No prior knowledge needed; this is the foundation for everything else in this dashboard.

🥃 Whisky — How It's Made

Whisky (spelt "whiskey" in the US and Ireland) is, at its purest, a spirit distilled from fermented grain and matured in wooden casks. The classic Scottish/Irish/American process runs through four stages:

1

Malting & Mashing

Barley (or other grain) is steeped in water to germinate, releasing enzymes that convert starch to sugar. The malted grain is milled and mixed with hot water to extract a sugary liquid called "wort."

2

Fermentation

Yeast is added to the wort, converting sugars into alcohol over 2–4 days, producing a beer-like liquid (~8% ABV) called "wash."

3

Distillation

The wash is heated in copper pot stills (Scotch malt whisky) or continuous column stills (grain whisky, bourbon), concentrating alcohol to 60–70% ABV and separating out flavour compounds.

4

Maturation

New-make spirit is aged in oak casks — typically ex-bourbon or ex-sherry casks for Scotch — for a legally mandated minimum (3 years in Scotland, 2 years in much of the EU) before it can legally be called "whisky."

🍇 Brandy — How It's Made

Brandy is a spirit distilled from fermented fruit juice — almost always grapes, though apple brandy (Calvados) and other fruit brandies exist. The process:

1

Fermentation

Grape juice (or wine) is fermented into a low-alcohol base wine, typically 8–12% ABV.

2

Double Distillation

In Cognac, France, the base wine is distilled twice in traditional copper pot stills ("alembic Charentais"), concentrating it to ~70% ABV. This double-pass process is central to Cognac's character.

3

Oak Ageing

The clear spirit is aged in French oak (typically Limousin or Tronçais) barrels for a minimum 2 years (Cognac VS) up to decades (XO), developing colour and complex flavour.

4

Blending

Master blenders combine eaux-de-vie of different ages and origins to achieve a consistent house style before bottling, usually diluted to 40% ABV.

India's brandy story is different and distinctive: Indian brandy (Mansion House, Honey Bee, Morpheus) is overwhelmingly ENA-based (see IMFL section below) rather than 100% grape-distilled, yet India is — by volume — among the largest brandy-consuming and brandy-producing markets in the world, with Mansion House Brandy alone ranking as the world's second-largest brandy brand by case volume.

🍺 Beer — How It's Made

Beer is fermented (not distilled) from malted cereal grain — usually barley — flavoured with hops. Because there's no distillation step, the process is shorter and the alcohol content much lower (typically 4–8% ABV):

1

Malting

Barley is germinated and kiln-dried to create malt, which provides the fermentable sugars and characteristic flavour/colour.

2

Mashing & Lautering

Malt is mashed with hot water to convert starches to sugar, then the sweet liquid ("wort") is separated from the spent grain.

3

Boiling with Hops

Wort is boiled with hops, which add bitterness, aroma, and act as a natural preservative.

4

Fermentation & Conditioning

Yeast converts sugar to alcohol and CO₂ over 1–3 weeks (ales ferment warmer/faster; lagers ferment cooler/slower), followed by a conditioning/maturation period before packaging.

Indian beer is overwhelmingly lager-style (Kingfisher, the dominant brand, is a pale lager), reflecting both colonial-era British brewing influence and consumer preference for a lighter, more refreshing style suited to India's climate — unlike Belgium or Germany, where ale and wheat-beer traditions are far more diverse.

🍷 Wine — How It's Made

Wine is simply fermented grape juice — no distillation, and traditionally minimal additional processing:

1

Harvest & Crush

Grapes are harvested (timing is critical — sugar/acid balance determines style) and crushed to release juice ("must").

2

Fermentation

Yeast converts grape sugars to alcohol over 1–4 weeks. For red wine, the skins ferment with the juice (giving colour/tannin); for white wine, skins are removed before fermentation.

3

Ageing & Clarification

Wine rests in stainless steel tanks (fresh, fruity styles) or oak barrels (structured, complex styles), then is filtered/fined for clarity.

4

Bottling

Finished wine (typically 11–15% ABV) is bottled, sometimes with further bottle-ageing before release.

India's wine industry is geographically concentrated almost entirely around Nashik, Maharashtra (Sula's home base) — a region with a viticultural climate broadly comparable to parts of Australia/California, with two harvests possible in some years. This is structurally different from Europe, where centuries-old Appellation/AOC systems (Bordeaux, Burgundy, Champagne) tightly regulate which grapes, yields, and methods can be used in a given named region.

🇮🇳 IMFL vs Country Liquor — India's Unique Classification

This is the single most important concept for understanding Indian alcobev investing, and it has no real Western equivalent:

IMFL — Indian-Made Foreign Liquor

Despite the name, IMFL has nothing to do with imports — it simply means Western-style spirits (whisky, brandy, rum, gin, vodka) manufactured in India. The category was named during British colonial rule to distinguish it from indigenous/traditional liquors. Most IMFL — including USL's McDowell's No.1, ABD's Officers Choice, and the bulk of the industry — is made not from 100% malted grain like Scotch, but from Extra Neutral Alcohol (ENA), a highly purified, nearly flavourless alcohol distilled from molasses (a sugarcane by-product) or, increasingly, grain. Flavouring, colouring (caramel), and blending give the final product its whisky/brandy/rum character. This is why Indian "whisky" historically could not legally be sold as whisky in the EU/UK without a minimum grain/malt content — a key reason companies like Radico, Tilaknagar, and Amrut have launched 100%-grain "Indian Single Malts" as a separate premium category to compete globally.

Country Liquor (CL)

Country Liquor is an even more India-specific, lower-price category — typically a plain or lightly spiced spirit made from molasses-based ENA, sold mainly in rural and value-conscious urban markets at a fraction of IMFL prices. It is taxed and regulated separately (often per "proof litre," a measure of alcohol strength) and dominates volumes in states like Maharashtra. GM Breweries is the listed sector's purest play on this category, manufacturing CL almost exclusively for the Maharashtra market. CL has no real Western parallel — the closest comparison is unbranded/own-label cheap spirits, but CL's scale, regulatory framework, and cultural embeddedness (especially in rural India) make it a distinct category of its own.
